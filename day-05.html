Day Five

Behold the Lamb

John 1:29  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, John the Baptist had been preaching the coming of one greater than himself, but when Jesus finally appeared, the title he chose was not "Conqueror" or "Lord" or even "Messiah" — it was "Lamb." Give us today the grace to understand why. Let the full weight of that word settle on us, and let us not flinch from what it means. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"The next day he saw Jesus coming toward him, and said, 'Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world!'"
John 1:29, ESV

John the Baptist was not a man given to understatement. He had preached in the desert with the fire of Elijah, dressed in camel's hair, eating locusts, leveling the proud and lifting the humble, declaring that one was coming whose sandal he was unworthy to fasten. All of that thunder — and then Jesus walks toward him along the river bank, and the single word John chooses is: Lamb.

Of all the titles available to him, this is the one he reaches for. Not King, though Jesus is that. Not Prophet, though Jesus is that. Not even Messiah, though every eye along the Jordan would have recognized the word and leaned in. John reaches back into the oldest and most blood-soaked strata of Israel's story and pulls out the most unlikely title of all: the Lamb of God.

To hear this phrase as a first-century Jew is to hear an entire library. There is the Passover lamb of Exodus 12 — without blemish, its blood marking the doorposts so that judgment passes over — the night when Israel was rescued from slavery through the death of something innocent in their place. There are the daily temple sacrifices of Exodus 29, the endless stream of animals offered morning and evening, century after century, the smoke rising day and night over Jerusalem as Israel's ongoing acknowledgment that sin has a cost. And beneath all of it, pressing up through the centuries like bedrock through topsoil, there is Isaiah 53: "He was led like a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before its shearers is silent, so he opened not his mouth."

John is saying: every one of those lambs was pointing at this one. Every drop of blood shed in the temple precincts across a thousand years of Israel's worship was a rehearsal for what is walking toward him now along the riverbank. He is not merely comparing Jesus to a lamb. He is declaring that Jesus is what every lamb was always meant to be — the real thing that all the types and shadows were straining to represent.

C.H. Spurgeon once pressed his congregation on the audacity of what follows: "who takes away the sin of the world." Not the sin of Israel only. Not the sin of the pious. The sin of the world. The scope is deliberately, almost recklessly, universal. The Lamb stands at the river and the announcement goes out: there is an offering sufficient for every sin ever committed by every person who has ever drawn breath. Not that every person will receive the benefit of it — but that the Lamb himself is adequate for all of it. He does not come to make a partial payment. He comes to make a sufficient one.

And notice the word takes away — the Greek airō carries the sense of lifting up and carrying away, of removing a thing from its place. This is not merely the forgiveness of sin as a legal accounting adjustment, a debit moved from one column to another. It is the actual, physical, irreversible carrying-away of guilt — borne in the body of the Lamb, removed from us, gone. The prophet Micah saw it coming: "You will cast all our sins into the depths of the sea" (Micah 7:19). The Lamb does not negotiate a reduced sentence. He removes the accusation entirely.

What is astonishing — and what should deepen our worship considerably — is the way this title coexists with what we have seen in the previous days. The one who upholds the universe by the word of his power (Hebrews 1:3). The one for whom all things were created (Colossians 1:16). The one who existed before time in the glory of the Father (John 17:5). This is the Lamb. The most powerful Person who has ever existed chose to come in the form of the most vulnerable creature in the sacrificial system. He did not overpower sin. He absorbed it. He did not outrun death. He went through it. He came not as a warrior who conquers by force, but as a Lamb who conquers by surrender — and in that surrender accomplishes what no force of arms could ever achieve.

Revelation 5 completes the picture: in heaven, at the center of the throne, surrounded by the worship of ten thousand times ten thousand, stands — a Lamb, as though it had been slain (Revelation 5:6). Even in glory, he bears the marks. Even in exaltation, he is still the Lamb. The sacrifice was not a chapter that closed at the resurrection. It is the eternal testimony to who he is and what he has done. And that Lamb is still calling: Behold. Look. This is what has been done for you.

For Reflection
  1. What does it mean to you that the most powerful Person in the universe chose to come in the form of a Lamb — that he conquered not through force but through surrender? What does this teach you about the nature of real power and real love?
  2. John says the Lamb "takes away" sin — present tense, ongoing, continuous action. How does this affect the way you bring your failures and fallings-short to Christ? Does it change anything to know the removal is real and permanent?
  3. If you have access to a Bible, read Isaiah 53:4–7 slowly. What strikes you most about how the prophet describes the suffering servant — written seven centuries before Christ arrived?

Going Deeper

The "Lamb of God" connects to the entire sacrificial logic of the Old Testament — which is itself a long, patient preparation for the one it was always pointing toward. The Passover lamb had to be without blemish (Exodus 12:5) not because the animal had any moral standing, but because it was a placeholder, a type, a signpost toward something the system itself could never accomplish. The writer of Hebrews makes this explicit: "It is impossible for the blood of bulls and goats to take away sins" (Hebrews 10:4). Every sacrifice offered under the old covenant was a promissory note, not the payment itself — a repeated acknowledgment of debt, not a discharge of it. Christ is the payment. His sinlessness is not incidental — it is what qualifies him as the unblemished Lamb. His death is not a tragedy that God repurposed — it is the purpose for which he came (Mark 10:45).

The theological term for what happened at the cross is penal substitutionary atonement — Christ bore in his own body the penalty our sin deserved, in our place, as our substitute. This is not the only dimension of what the atonement accomplishes, but it is foundational to the others. The Lamb metaphor captures it precisely: an innocent life given in the place of the guilty, so that justice is satisfied and the guilty go free. That the same Lamb appears in Revelation 5 at the center of heaven's throne — receiving worship from every creature in creation — tells us that the sacrifice was accepted, the offering honored, the mission complete. The one who was slain is the one who reigns. And he reigns wearing the marks of what he endured to bring you home.
