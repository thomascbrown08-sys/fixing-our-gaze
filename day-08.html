Day Eight

I AM

John 8:58 & Exodus 3:14  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Holy God, when Moses asked your name at the burning bush, you answered with a statement of pure, unqualified being — "I AM WHO I AM" — a name that refuses to be contained, conditioned, or dependent on anything outside itself. And now your Son stands before his enemies and speaks the same words. Let their weight fall on us today not merely as a doctrinal claim to be assessed but as a living Person to be worshiped. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Jesus said to them, 'Truly, truly, I say to you, before Abraham was, I am.'"

"God said to Moses, 'I AM WHO I AM.' And he said, 'Say this to the people of Israel: I AM has sent me to you.'"
John 8:58 & Exodus 3:14, ESV

The Pharisees have been pressing Jesus about his identity. He has spoken of Abraham, and they have thrown it back at him: You are not yet fifty years old, and have you seen Abraham? The question is meant to expose an absurdity. And Jesus' answer does expose an absurdity — but not the one they expected.

Before Abraham was, I am.

The grammar is deliberately, scandalously wrong, and everyone in the room knows it. The proper form would be: "Before Abraham was, I was." But Jesus does not say I was. He says I am — present tense, absolute, unconditioned. He is not simply claiming to have existed before Abraham. He is claiming to be the one who simply and always is — without past, without future, without dependence on any moment of time. The one who spoke to Moses from the burning bush, the one who told Israel to call him simply I AM, the one whose name in Hebrew — YHWH — is rooted in the verb "to be." That one. That eternal, self-existing, uncontainable one. That is who stands before you.

The crowd understands him exactly. They do not say "what a curious grammatical choice." They pick up stones (John 8:59). In first-century Judaism, the penalty for blasphemy was stoning — and to apply the divine name to yourself was the apex of blasphemy, unless you were the one to whom the name actually belongs. The Pharisees' response is not an overreaction. It is the only rational response to the claim, if the claim is false. C.S. Lewis pressed this point memorably: a person who said what Jesus said is either the Lord of all, or a dangerous madman. There is no comfortable middle position. The stones are the honest response to a lie; worship is the honest response to the truth.

To hear "I AM" as a first-century Jewish listener is to hear it embedded in a whole history. The name YHWH — rendered "LORD" in most English translations — appears in the Old Testament nearly seven thousand times. It is the name God gave Moses at the burning bush when Israel was enslaved and crying out and God had heard them and was about to come down (Exodus 3:7–8). This name is not primarily a philosophical statement about divine ontology, though it is that. It is first a covenant name — the name of the God who comes, who hears, who acts, who delivers. The self-existing one is also the one who stoops into human suffering to rescue.

And this is the Jesus who stands before the Pharisees in John 8. The eternal I AM has a face and a voice and dusty sandals. The one who told Moses I have surely seen the affliction of my people, I have heard their cry (Exodus 3:7) has now come in person — not to send a representative, not to issue instructions from a distance, but to become one of the afflicted himself and carry the rescue through from the inside.

Throughout John's Gospel, Jesus rings variations on the divine name in seven great declarations: I am the bread of life; I am the light of the world; I am the door; I am the good shepherd; I am the resurrection and the life; I am the way and the truth and the life; I am the true vine. These are not merely poetic metaphors. They are the I AM naming himself for each human need — saying, in effect: whatever you most require, I am that. The name that burned in the bush now illumines every dimension of human want and suffering.

This is why the God of Exodus and the Christ of the Gospels are not two different deities, as if the New Testament introduces someone new. They are one — the eternal I AM, Father and Son and Spirit, the same God who spoke to Abraham, delivered Israel, and filled the temple with his glory, now standing in sandals before the Pharisees with the divine name on his lips. What appeared in shadow and symbol across fifteen centuries of Israel's history has become, in this moment, fully and finally visible. Before Abraham was — before the bush burned, before the first sacrifice, before the first prophecy pointed forward — he simply, eternally, is.

For Reflection
  1. The crowd's response was to reach for stones — because they understood exactly what Jesus was claiming. The claim is inescapable; it demands a response. How does "Before Abraham was, I am" press you toward a particular response today?
  2. Jesus connects himself to the God who came specifically to hear the cries of enslaved people and deliver them (Exodus 3:7–8). What does this tell you about the character of the one who bears the divine name — and about what kind of God you are approaching when you pray?
  3. The seven "I am" statements in John each address a specific human need. Which one do you most need to receive today — and what would it mean to actually receive it as true?

Going Deeper

The divine name YHWH — the Tetragrammaton — derives from the Hebrew verb hayah, "to be" or "to become," and is typically translated "LORD" in English Bibles to honor the ancient Jewish practice of not pronouncing the name aloud. When God tells Moses his name is ehyeh asher ehyeh — "I AM WHO I AM" or "I WILL BE WHAT I WILL BE" — he is revealing two things simultaneously: his absolute self-existence (he depends on nothing and no one for his being) and his dynamic covenantal faithfulness (he will be present, he will come through, he will be what his people need him to be). The name is both ontological and relational.

Jesus' use of the same formula in John 8:58 does more than claim equality with God in the abstract. It specifically claims to be the covenanting, coming, delivering God of Israel's whole history — the one who has been faithful across every generation. This is why John's Gospel can begin with the language of Genesis 1:1 and end with Thomas's confession, "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28). The entire Gospel is the unveiling of who the I AM truly is. And the Reformed tradition's insistence on the unity of the Old and New Testaments — that the God who called Abraham, delivered Israel, and dwelt in the temple is the same God revealed in Jesus Christ — finds its sharpest and most irreducible expression in these five English words: Before Abraham was, I am.
