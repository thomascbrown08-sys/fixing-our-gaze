Day Nine

Full of Grace and Truth

John 1:14, 16–17  ·  New King James Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, we confess that we have often reduced you to a single dimension — all grace with no truth, or all truth with no grace. We have shaped you into a reflection of our own preferences and made peace with a smaller Christ than the one the Gospels present. Break the idol. Show us your actual face — full of both, without compromise of either. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And the Word became flesh and dwelt among us, and we beheld His glory, the glory as of the only begotten of the Father, full of grace and truth… And of His fullness we have all received, and grace for grace. For the law was given through Moses, but grace and truth came through Jesus Christ."
John 1:14, 16–17, NKJV

We return today to the verse that opened our first day — the Incarnation in John 1:14 — but now we linger over two words we passed by too quickly: grace and truth. They appear here as twin descriptors of Christ's character, inseparable in him, rarely held together so fully in any of us.

Most of us, when we examine our instincts honestly, find that we favor one over the other. There are those among us who lean toward grace: warm, accepting, slow to say a hard thing, eager to believe the best, generous to a fault. In their presence, people feel welcomed. But sometimes the welcome extends to things that should not be welcomed — to sin reframed as struggle, to brokenness given a comfortable name, to a God who is fond of everyone regardless of what anyone does. This is not grace. This is sentimentality wearing grace's clothes.

And there are those who lean toward truth: clear, honest, precise, unwilling to fudge what Scripture says, concerned for the integrity of the message. In their presence, you know exactly where you stand. But sometimes this clarity becomes a kind of cold light that illuminates without warming, that sees sin accurately but cannot seem to hold the sinner tenderly alongside the diagnosis. This is not truth at its fullest. It is truth without the flesh — disembodied, and therefore not quite whole.

Christ, John says, is full of both. The Greek word — plērēs — does not mean a careful balance, as if he contains a measured dose of each and must be careful not to let one overflow the other. It means overflow. Abundance. He is so full of grace that it pours out. So full of truth that no honest question frightens him, no hidden sin surprises him, no evasion survives in his presence. And these two fullnesses do not compete; they compound. His grace is truthful grace — which means it takes your sin seriously enough to address it rather than excuse it. His truth is gracious truth — which means it tells you what is real about yourself in a voice that has already decided to redeem you.

What follows is among the most lavish images in the New Testament: "of His fullness we have all received, and grace for grace." The phrase charin anti charitos — grace for grace, or grace upon grace — pictures a wave that breaks on the shore and is replaced by another wave, and another, and another, without interruption, without reduction, without ever running to the end of the supply. You do not draw from a reserve of grace that is slowly being depleted. You draw from a Person who is inexhaustibly full.

Then John introduces a contrast that is easily misread: "The law was given through Moses, but grace and truth came through Jesus Christ." This is not a dismissal of Moses or of the law. Paul will later say the law is holy, righteous, and good (Romans 7:12). The law revealed what God requires of human beings, and in doing so revealed how deep the chasm between what is required and what we produce actually is. Moses was given something real and glorious. But what Moses was given was the diagnosis; what Jesus brings is the cure. The law shows what grace must accomplish. Christ is the accomplishing of it.

Dietrich Bonhoeffer's great warning against cheap grace — grace that costs nothing and changes nothing, that offers forgiveness without repentance and absolution without discipleship — is a call to understand that genuine grace is not warm sentiment. It is the costliest love in the universe, the love that looked at the full truth of your condition and did not avert its eyes but moved toward you at immense expense. Christ's truth-telling about sin is not a withdrawal of grace; it is grace taking you seriously enough to tell you what is actually wrong and then doing something about it. He is full of both. And in him — in his abundance, his inexhaustible overflow — there is enough of both for you.

For Reflection
  1. In your experience of Christianity — in churches you have attended, communities you have inhabited, or your own inner life — has the emphasis tilted toward grace (accepting and warm but sometimes unclear about sin) or truth (clear and precise but sometimes cold)? What has been the effect of that imbalance?
  2. "Grace upon grace" pictures a supply that is never depleted. What would it mean to live as someone drawing from an inexhaustible source rather than rationing your spiritual resources — as though the well might eventually run dry?
  3. How is Christ's truth-telling about sin actually a form of grace — how is his honesty about what is broken in us a gift rather than a threat? Has there been a moment when an honest word spoken in love changed something in you?

Going Deeper

John 1:14's phrase "full of grace and truth" echoes a recurring description of God in the Hebrew Old Testament: hesed ve'emet — steadfast love (often translated "lovingkindness" or "mercy") and faithfulness (truth, reliability). This is the covenant character of YHWH as declared in Exodus 34:6, when God passes before Moses on the mountain and proclaims his own name: "The LORD, the LORD, a God merciful and gracious, slow to anger, and abounding in steadfast love and faithfulness." Crucially, this self-disclosure happens just after the golden calf disaster — immediately after Israel's most spectacular act of covenant betrayal. God reveals the fullness of his grace and truth precisely to a people who have just proven they deserve neither.

John is making a breathtaking claim: the hesed ve'emet that defined YHWH's covenant character throughout the Old Testament has now become flesh. The one who proclaimed his own name to Moses on the mountain is now walking among his people with dusty feet. And the pattern of that grace is determinative: God's fullness is revealed not in spite of human failure but in response to it. This is not cheap grace — the golden calf is not dismissed; Moses must intercede, and the consequences are real. But grace is more powerful than the failure. The same is true in Christ. He does not arrive when we have become deserving. He arrives because we are not. That is what grace upon grace means: wave after wave, from a God who abounds.
