Day Eleven

The Son the Father Loves

John 3:35  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, we have seen your Son in his cosmic glory and his eternal preexistence. Today draw our gaze toward something quieter and more intimate: the love that passes between you and your Son from before time began. Let us understand that this love is not a theological fact to be catalogued but a living reality to be marveled at — and one that, in Christ, has extended itself to include us. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"The Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand."
John 3:35, ESV

John 3:35 is almost overlooked — a single sentence tucked between the famous John 3:16 and the warning of verse 36, in the middle of John the Baptist's final testimony. Most readers pass through it on the way to something else. But it contains, in ten words, the primal reality from which everything else in the gospel flows.

The Father loves the Son. Not past tense, not conditional, not occasional. Present. Active. Continuous. Before creation, before redemption, before any decision God ever made about the world — the love between Father and Son is the foundational fact. The universe does not stand beneath God's love as its ultimate context; God's love for his Son is what the universe stands beneath.

The verb here is agapaō — the same word John uses in the verse just before this one, in what is perhaps the most quoted sentence in all of Scripture: "God so loved the world" (John 3:16). It is the word for a love that chooses its object and commits to it absolutely, a love that is not a response to the beloved's worthiness but the expression of the lover's character. When the Father loves the Son with this love, he is doing what he has always done, what he will always do, what could not be otherwise without ceasing to be God.

Jonathan Edwards, in his meditations on the Trinity, describes the inner life of God as a society of love so perfect and so full that it overflows. The Father's love for the Son is so complete that it generates — eternally, without beginning — a third Person: the Spirit, the love itself subsisting between them. This is not a static system. It is a dynamic, overflowing, always-generating life. Creation, Edwards suggests, is itself an expression of this overflowing: a God so full of love could not, in a sense, not make a world — not because he needed to, but because love tends to expand its circle.

And then the second clause: "and has given all things into his hand." The love is not passive. It pours itself out in gift. The Father's love for the Son expresses itself in total entrustment — not partial authority, not delegated power with conditions attached, but all things. Everything the Father has, he has given to the Son (John 16:15; 17:10). The one who is loved is also the one who receives everything as an expression of that love.

This is the relationship from which the Incarnation flows. When the Son comes to earth, he does not come on a private initiative or an unauthorized mission. He comes as the Father's beloved, sent in love, to fulfill what love requires. Every act of compassion Jesus performs — the touched leper, the raised widow's son, the forgiven adulteress — is the love of the Father for the Son now turned outward toward the broken world the Son has entered. The love at the center of the Trinity does not remain contained within it.

And then there is what John 17:26 does with this love — the extraordinary word Jesus prays on the night of his arrest: "I made known to them your name, and I will continue to make it known, that the love with which you have loved me may be in them, and I in them." The love the Father has for the Son — this primal, eternal, foundational love — is what Jesus is asking to be poured into his people. Not a reflection of it. Not a diminished copy. The love itself, by the Spirit, in us. This is what adoption means: not merely legal standing, but participation. We do not observe the love of the Trinity from a distance. In Christ, by the Spirit, we are drawn into it.

For Reflection
  1. "The Father loves the Son and has given all things into his hand." Authority and love are connected here — all that the Father has flows to the Son as an expression of love, not as a power transfer. What does it tell you about the nature of authority when it originates in love rather than conquest?
  2. Jonathan Edwards imagined the inner life of God as a society of love so full it overflows into creation. If creation itself is an expression of the love within the Trinity, how does that change the way you see the physical world around you — the sky, a friend's face, a shared meal?
  3. Jesus prays in John 17:26 that the love the Father has for him would be "in" his people. How does this change what you understand God to be doing in you? Not merely forgiving, not merely instructing — but drawing you into the love that constitutes his own inner life?

Going Deeper

The love between Father and Son is not a metaphor accommodated to human understanding — it is the eternal, ontological reality that underlies everything else. Reformed theologians in the tradition of John Owen have spoken of the "eternal generation of the Son" as the technical description of the Father's eternal, personal self-communication in the Son: not a temporal act, not a creation event, but the eternal mode of the Son's existence in relation to the Father (John 1:18; 5:26). The Son is "from" the Father eternally — not sequentially, not causally in any creaturely sense, but in the way that the sun's radiance is "from" the sun without ever having a moment of not-yet-shining.

This matters for understanding our adoption. When the New Testament says that we have received "the spirit of adoption as sons, by whom we cry, 'Abba! Father!'" (Romans 8:15), and that we are "heirs of God and fellow heirs with Christ" (Romans 8:17), it is asserting something audacious: that the relationship of the eternal Son to the Father has been opened to include us — not by our nature, but by grace, through union with the Son who has made us his brothers and sisters (Hebrews 2:11). Our adoption is not a legal fiction. It is, by the Spirit, a real participation in the love that the Father has for the Son. The love John 3:35 describes has been extended — at the cost of the cross — to include you.
