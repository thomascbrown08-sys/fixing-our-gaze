Day Twelve

The Ancient of Days

Daniel 7:13–14  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, Daniel saw visions of things still to come, and what he saw left him shaken and undone. Yet what terrified the prophet is for us a word of solid hope: the Son of Man has come, has been presented before the Father, and has received a kingdom that will never be destroyed. Give us eyes today to see what is actually happening — in history, in our lives, in the unseen heavens — through the lens of this ancient vision. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"I saw in the night visions, and behold, with the clouds of heaven there came one like a son of man, and he came to the Ancient of Days and was presented before him. And to him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom, that all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him; his dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom one that shall not be destroyed."
Daniel 7:13–14, ESV

Of all the titles Jesus could have chosen for himself — Rabbi, Prophet, Son of David, Messiah — the one he reaches for most often is "the Son of Man." It appears more than eighty times in the Gospels, always on his own lips, and it puzzles readers who assume it is a title of humility. It is not. It is the most audacious thing he says.

Every synagogue-educated person who heard it would have heard Daniel 7 behind it — this night vision of thrones set in place, books opened, the Ancient of Days seated in blinding white, and then this figure: "one like a son of man," coming on the clouds of heaven, approaching the divine throne not to be judged but to be invested. To him was given dominion and glory and a kingdom. This is a coronation scene, a royal investiture. The Son of Man does not seize power; he receives it, from the Father, as a gift and a right.

When Jesus calls himself the Son of Man in the hearing of first-century Jews, he is making an unmistakable claim: he is the one Daniel saw. He is the one to whom universal, eternal dominion belongs. And he is making this claim while walking the dusty roads of Galilee, eating with sinners, touching lepers, and healing the sick — which is either madness or the most stunning truth ever spoken.

The ascension is the fulfillment of Daniel 7. When Jesus rises into the cloud in Acts 1:9, Luke is not describing a rocket launch into the sky. He is describing the Son of Man going where Daniel saw him go — into the presence of the Ancient of Days, to receive the kingdom. The ascension is not a withdrawal; it is an enthronement. Christ did not leave when he ascended. He began, in a new mode, to reign. "He is not here," the angel said at the empty tomb. But "not here" does not mean absent. It means present everywhere, and present in authority, at the right hand of the Father (Ephesians 1:20–21), with all things put under his feet (1 Corinthians 15:25).

The scope of the dominion Daniel describes is universal: "all peoples, nations, and languages should serve him." This is not a modest regional claim. This is the Son of Man's dominion stretching to every corner of the earth — which is the theological ground of the Great Commission (Matthew 28:18–20). Jesus does not say "Go make disciples of all nations because I hope someday to have authority over them." He says it because the authority has already been given. The mission of the church is not to conquer but to announce what has already happened: the Son of Man has received the kingdom, and his kingdom is open to every people under heaven.

"His dominion is an everlasting dominion, which shall not pass away, and his kingdom one that shall not be destroyed." Every empire in recorded history has risen and fallen. The Babylonian empire that surrounded Daniel is dust. Rome is a ruin. The greatest powers of the modern world will one day be archives and footnotes. The kingdom of the Son of Man is the only one in history about which such a promise can honestly be made — and it has already been given, not as an aspiration but as a decree. He reigns now. And he will reign when every other reign has ended.

N.T. Wright observes that the resurrection and ascension are not a reversal of the cross but its vindication — the Father's public declaration that the crucified one was in the right, and the powers that crucified him were in the wrong. The one the world threw away is the one the Ancient of Days has enthroned. Which means the kingdom is upside-down by every ordinary measure: the rejected stone is the cornerstone, the executed criminal is the everlasting King.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus chose "Son of Man" — with all its Danielic freight — as his most frequent self-designation. What does it tell you about his self-understanding that he was thinking of this vision throughout his earthly ministry, even while healing and teaching and suffering?
  2. The ascension is not a departure but an enthronement — the moment Daniel 7 is fulfilled. How does thinking of Christ not as withdrawn but as actively reigning right now change how you pray, or how you face circumstances that seem out of control?
  3. Every earthly kingdom passes away; Christ's does not. Where in your life do you find yourself anchoring hopes or security in something that is, in the long run, temporary? What would it look like to anchor those things instead to the one kingdom that cannot be shaken?

Going Deeper

Daniel 7 is one of the key texts for understanding both Jesus' self-understanding and the early church's Christology. The "one like a son of man" who approaches the Ancient of Days is a deliberately enigmatic figure — clearly human in form, yet able to approach the divine throne and receive universal, eternal dominion. The early church read it consistently as a messianic prophecy fulfilled in Jesus' death, resurrection, and ascension. Importantly, the vision establishes that Christ's reign is present, not merely future.

Reformed eschatology — particularly in the amillennial and postmillennial traditions — has emphasized this "already" dimension: Christ is reigning now, at the right hand of the Father (Ephesians 1:20–21), with all things put under his feet (1 Corinthians 15:25–27). The "not yet" of his full consummation and the visible defeat of all enemies awaits his return; but the "already" of his enthronement is the present reality in which the church lives and from which it proclaims the gospel. This is why the Commission in Matthew 28 begins with an indicative before it reaches an imperative: "All authority in heaven and on earth has been given to me" — that is the fact; "therefore go" — that is the command. The mission rests entirely on the reality of the present reign.
