Day Fourteen

The Anointed One

Isaiah 61:1–2a & Luke 4:21  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, you anointed prophets and priests and kings throughout the long centuries of Israel's waiting. And in each anointing there was an implicit promise: one day, the Anointed One will come — not merely a prophet, not merely a priest, not merely a king, but all three in one Person, once and finally. We stand on this side of that arrival. Help us not to walk past it. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"The Spirit of the Lord GOD is upon me, because the LORD has anointed me to bring good news to the poor; he has sent me to bind up the brokenhearted, to proclaim liberty to the captives, and the opening of the prison to those who are bound; to proclaim the year of the LORD's favor…"

"Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing."
Isaiah 61:1–2a, ESV  ·  Luke 4:21, ESV

The synagogue in Nazareth. The Sabbath. Jesus stands to read. He unrolls the scroll of Isaiah and finds this passage — not randomly, almost certainly deliberately. He reads it. He rolls the scroll, hands it back to the attendant, and sits down. Every eye in the room is fixed on him. And then: "Today this Scripture has been fulfilled in your hearing."

Four words of Scripture — Christ in Greek, Messiah in Hebrew — both mean the same thing: the Anointed One. And anointing in Israel was the act by which someone was set apart and equipped by God for a particular office. Prophets were anointed (1 Kings 19:16). Priests were anointed (Exodus 29:7). Kings were anointed (1 Samuel 16:13). Each was a partial, preparatory anointing — a person selected and equipped for one dimension of God's governing work in the world. The prophet spoke. The priest interceded. The king ruled. None of the three held all three offices simultaneously.

Jesus is the one anointed to hold all three — simultaneously, permanently, and completely. In the synagogue at Nazareth, in a single public moment, he announces this. The Spirit who descended on him at the Jordan has anointed him, Isaiah said, for a specific mission: good news to the poor, binding up the brokenhearted, liberty to captives, the opening of prisons, the year of the LORD's favor.

That last phrase — the "year of the LORD's favor" — points to the Jubilee of Leviticus 25: the great reset appointed every fifty years in Israel, when all debts were cancelled, all slaves freed, all land returned to its original families. It was an economic and social revolution built into the calendar, an acknowledgment that no arrangement of wealth and power was permanent, that every human structure stood under the judgment of God's ordering. And it was also a promissory note — pointing toward something no economic system could actually accomplish: the cancellation of the deepest debt, the freeing of the most fundamental captivity.

Jesus declares that the ultimate Jubilee has arrived. He has come not to announce it as distant but to enact it as present. In him, the blind receive sight not merely as a medical event but as a symbol of the deeper illumination he brings. The captives released are not only those in Roman prisons but those bound by sin, shame, and the fear of death (John 8:34; Hebrews 2:15). The poor who receive good news are not only those without material resources but those who know — as the Beatitudes will shortly clarify — their own absolute bankruptcy before God (Matthew 5:3).

Tim Keller observed that Jesus fulfills Isaiah 61 in a way that simultaneously addresses material and spiritual poverty, because the root of all poverty and captivity — the rupture between humanity and God — is what he has ultimately come to address. This is not a spiritualization that ignores real suffering. It is a recognition that no merely political or economic program can reach the deepest level of what is wrong. The Anointed One does not choose between caring for bodies and saving souls. He does both, because both belong to the human being he has come to restore.

The room in Nazareth is silent after he says it. Then the astonishment begins, then the questioning, then the rage — because the claims he is making are either the most welcome words ever spoken or the most outrageous. He does not give anyone the option of a comfortable middle reaction. Today this is fulfilled. Not someday. Not in principle. Today, in your hearing, in your synagogue, in the person standing before you now.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus chose this passage from Isaiah for his first public declaration of his mission. What does this choice tell you about what he understood himself to have come to do — and about what matters most to him?
  2. The three offices — Prophet, Priest, King — each address a different dimension of human need: our ignorance of God, our guilt before God, and the power of sin and death over us. Which of these do you most need from Christ right now?
  3. The Jubilee cancels debts, frees slaves, and restores what was lost. If you were to name honestly one thing in your own life that most needs Christ's Jubilee — one captivity, one debt, one loss — what would it be? And what would it mean to bring it to the Anointed One?

Going Deeper

The Reformed tradition's doctrine of the "threefold office" (munus triplex) of Christ — Prophet, Priest, and King — was developed systematically by Calvin and has been a cornerstone of Reformed Christology ever since. Each office addresses a distinct consequence of the fall: as Prophet, Christ overcomes our ignorance and blindness; as Priest, he addresses our guilt and the alienation from God that guilt produces; as King, he overcomes the power of sin, death, and Satan over us. The glory of Isaiah 61 is that all three offices are announced simultaneously in a single moment in a Galilean synagogue.

This is not coincidental — it is the concentrated arrival of everything Israel's institutions had been preparing for. The prophets spoke and went silent; the priests offered and the sacrifices repeated endlessly; the kings rose and fell. The Anointed One does not speak and go silent. He does not offer repeatedly. He does not fall. His anointing is unique, permanent, and complete — offered once for all (Hebrews 7:27; 9:12), interceding always (Hebrews 7:25), reigning until every enemy is placed beneath his feet (1 Corinthians 15:25). The Jubilee he announced is not a temporary debt relief. It is the permanent restructuring of every relationship between God and humanity, accomplished once at the cross and applied continuously by the Spirit to all who are in him.
