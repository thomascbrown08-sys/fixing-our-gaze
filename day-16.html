Day Sixteen

Born of a Woman

Galatians 4:4–5  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, for fifteen days we have stood outside of time and gazed at your Son in his eternal glory — his preexistence, his cosmic supremacy, his divine names, his ancient love. Today we turn with wonder to the moment when that eternity entered time with a particular, irreversible specificity: born of a woman, in a province of Rome, in the fullness of a moment you had been preparing for centuries. The infinite accepted the finite. Let the weight of this landing shape everything we see in the days ahead. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"But when the fullness of time had come, God sent forth his Son, born of woman, born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law, so that we might receive adoption as sons."
Galatians 4:4–5, ESV

We have spent fifteen days standing, as it were, outside of history — gazing at the Son in his eternal nature, his preexistence, his cosmic supremacy, his divine names and attributes. It is right that we began there. To understand what happens next, we need to know what and who it is that happened. The Word made flesh is not a great human being who was elevated to divine status; he is the eternal Son who descended into human form. The Incarnation is descent, not ascent.

Now, having fixed our gaze on who he is in himself, we turn to how he lived among us. The arc changes here — without losing the grandeur of what we have seen, we enter the neighborhood where he pitched his tent, and we follow him in. The servant who came. The friend of the broken. The teacher whose words were like nothing the world had heard. The one who wept, and touched the leper, and ate with the wrong people, and refused to be what anyone expected.

Paul's compressed summary in Galatians 4:4–5 is the hinge. Three phrases, each carrying an enormous weight. The fullness of time. Born of woman. Born under the law. Together they describe not only what happened but why the timing and the manner of the happening matter so much.

The fullness of time. Not an arbitrary moment, not a random entry point. The Roman Empire had, in the century before Christ's birth, accomplished something no power in history had managed before: it had unified the Mediterranean world with roads, a common legal system, and a shared language — the koine Greek in which every New Testament letter would be written and every gospel would circulate. Jewish diaspora communities had seeded synagogues from Rome to Babylon, building networks of people already familiar with the Hebrew Scriptures and already waiting. The infrastructure for the gospel's spread had been assembled, over centuries, by a God who wastes nothing and is late for nothing. The fullness of time means: everything was ready. And then he came.

Born of woman. Two words, enormous weight. Not "appeared" or "materialized" or "descended." Born. The eternal Son passed through the entirety of ordinary human development: an embryo, an infant unable to lift his own head, a child learning to speak, a boy apprenticed to his father's trade, a young man in an occupied land under an occupying empire. He borrowed a body — which means he also borrowed a mother, borrowed hunger, borrowed the need for sleep, borrowed grief and laughter and the weariness that comes after a long day of walking. He did not experience these things from the outside. He experienced them.

Born under the law. He entered our exact legal and moral condition. He did not stand above the covenant obligations of Israel, observing from a safe distance. He submitted to circumcision, to the Torah, to the full weight of the covenant. And he did so not because he owed this as the eternal Son — he did not — but because we owed it and could not pay it. He entered our condition precisely so that his obedience, accumulated across an entire human life, could be credited to those who had accumulated only failure.

The twin purposes Paul names are worth pausing over together: to redeem and to give adoption. Redemption is the buying back of what was sold into bondage — freedom, at a price. Adoption is the receiving of the freed slave not merely as a free person but as a family member, an heir. He did not come merely to cancel the penalty. He came to remake the relationship. You are not, if you are in Christ, merely pardoned. You are brought in. The condemned prisoner is not only released; he is given the name of the judge's own family. This is what the fullness of time was full of: the moment when God's love could do both.

For Reflection
  1. "The fullness of time" suggests God was preparing the world for Christ's arrival for centuries — road networks, a common language, dispersed synagogues, a waiting people. Where in your own story do you see God preparing things — people, circumstances, losses, encounters — before you could understand why?
  2. "Born of woman, born under the law" — Jesus submitted to conditions he had no personal obligation to accept. He was born under a law he had no need to fulfill for his own sake. What does this willingness — this descent into our exact situation — tell you about the character of the one who came?
  3. The twin purposes are redemption and adoption: not merely pardoned but brought into the family. How does being a child of God rather than merely a forgiven offender change how you relate to God — and how you relate to other believers?

Going Deeper

Galatians 4:4–5 is Paul's most compressed statement of the Incarnation's purpose, and it hinges on the phrase "born under the law, to redeem those who were under the law." This is the logic of what the Reformed tradition calls the active obedience of Christ — a doctrine precious to Reformed soteriology and sometimes contested elsewhere. Christ's saving work is not merely his death (his passive obedience, his bearing of sin's penalty). It includes also his entire life of perfect, glad obedience to the law on our behalf.

What is credited to believers through justification is not merely the forgiveness of their lawbreaking, but the positive credit of Christ's law-keeping — his righteousness reckoned as ours (2 Corinthians 5:21; Romans 5:19). Paul's language in Philippians 3:9 makes this explicit: he wants to be found in Christ, possessing "not a righteousness of my own that comes from the law, but that which comes through faith in Christ, the righteousness from God that depends on faith." This is the righteousness of a life fully lived in obedience to everything the law required — lived not by Paul, but by Christ, and given to Paul. "Born under the law" matters precisely because it anchors his obedience to our condition: he was not born above it, watching it from a distance. He was born into it, bound by it, subject to every demand it made — so that every demand it makes of us could be met by him, in our name, on our behalf. His life is as much a gift as his death.
