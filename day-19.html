Day Nineteen

Tempted in Every Way

Hebrews 4:15  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you know what it is to be hungry and to be offered what would satisfy — at a cost that would compromise everything. You know what it is to be offered an easier path than the one the Father has set. You know the force of temptation not from a distance but from within the skin. You overcame it, and you overcame it for us. Let that victory be our confidence today, not our own track record. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin."
Hebrews 4:15, ESV

The Spirit who descended on Jesus at his baptism immediately drove him into the wilderness. Mark uses an almost violent word — ekballō, the same verb used for casting out demons. The beloved Son, publicly declared and anointed, is at once pressed into the place of testing. This is not accidental. It is the shape of the story: the pattern of beloved-son, declaration, and wilderness runs through Adam, through Israel, and now through Jesus — and only one of the three will emerge without having broken under the weight.

Forty days. A number that echoes deliberately. Israel spent forty years in the wilderness and failed repeatedly (Deuteronomy 9:24). Moses spent forty days on the mountain and came down with the law Israel would break almost immediately. Jesus spends forty days in the wilderness, and when the adversary comes with his tests, he does not break once.

The three temptations in Matthew 4 follow a pattern that is worth tracing. Each one begins with the assumption of his identity — "If you are the Son of God" — and then invites him to act from that identity in a way that bypasses the Father. Make bread from stones: use your power to meet your own need, rather than waiting on the Father to provide. Throw yourself from the temple and let angels catch you: secure the recognition you deserve through spectacle, rather than through the slow, hidden path the Father has appointed. Accept dominion over all the kingdoms: take what is rightfully yours now, through the adversary's shortcut, rather than through the cross the Father has ordained. Each temptation offers something real — bread, safety, authority — through a path that excludes the Father.

Jesus answers every temptation with words from Deuteronomy — Israel's foundational covenant text. He defeats the adversary not with a display of raw divine power but with received, trusted Scripture. This is theologically significant: he wins the way any human being is equipped to win, through dependence on the word of God and trust in the Father's provision. He does not win because it was easy for the Son of God. He wins because he chose, at every point, not to step outside the conditions of genuine human obedience.

And this is what Hebrews 4:15 builds on: he has been tempted as we are, yet without sin. He was not merely subjected to mild provocations that a divine being could brush off without feeling them. He was genuinely, severely tempted — the full force of what the adversary could bring, applied to a genuinely human nature that had been fasting for forty days. He felt it. He knows what the weight of it is from the inside, not from theological description. Which means that when you come to him today with your besetting sin, your exhaustion with the fight, your sense that the temptation is too strong for you — you come to someone who has been in the same fight.

The word in Hebrews is sympathize — literally, to suffer with. Not to observe sympathetically from a distance. To have been in the same condition, to know it from within. He is not a high priest who surveys our weakness from the calm of an unfallen nature, untouched by the pull of what tempts us. He stood where we stand. He felt what we feel. And he held.

Yet without sin — three words that do not diminish his sympathy but rather ground his ability to help. He is not a fellow sufferer who also failed and can only commiserate. He is the one who stood where we stand and did not fall, which means he can offer us, from the other side of the temptation, what we need to stand. His victory is not hoarded. It is offered. Come boldly to the throne of grace, Hebrews continues (4:16), that you may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need. The high priest who has been there is the same one who now intercedes. He is not remote. He knows exactly what you need.

For Reflection
  1. Each of the three temptations offered something genuinely desirable — bread, safety, authority — through a path that excluded the Father. What are the temptations in your own life that follow this pattern: a real good offered through a means that bypasses God's way?
  2. Jesus answered every temptation with received Scripture — not with raw willpower or divine power unilaterally exercised, but with the word of God trusted and applied. What does this tell you about the role of Scripture in the actual moment of moral pressure, not just in general spiritual formation?
  3. He sympathizes with our weakness because he has been in the same fight. What difference does it make to bring your struggles and failures to someone who has been there — rather than to a distant, untested, or merely theoretical deity?

Going Deeper

The Temptation narrative carries within it a typological logic that runs through all of Scripture: the second Adam succeeds where the first Adam failed. Adam was placed in a garden, given dominion, and tested — and yielded (Genesis 3). Israel was taken through the wilderness, given the law, and tested over forty years — and repeatedly failed. Jesus enters the wilderness, is subjected to the same categories of testing, and does not break once. This is what Paul draws on in Romans 5:12–21 and 1 Corinthians 15:45–49: Christ is the new Adam, whose obedience reverses the trajectory of the first Adam's disobedience. Where the first Adam's failure brought condemnation to all who are in him, the second Adam's obedience brings justification and life to all who are in him.

But the Hebrews text adds an indispensable dimension: the Temptation is not merely about typological fulfillment or legal obedience on our behalf. It is about high-priestly sympathy and ongoing intercession. A priest who has never suffered cannot fully intercede for those who suffer. Christ's experience of temptation — real, severe, costly, fully felt — equips him to intercede with specific knowledge of what we face (Hebrews 2:18). He is not a sympathizer from a distance. He is an interceding high priest who has been exactly where we are. And because he stood there without falling, the resource he brings us from that standing is not theoretical comfort but the actual power of proven obedience — extended to us in grace, available to us now, at the throne of grace, whenever we come.
