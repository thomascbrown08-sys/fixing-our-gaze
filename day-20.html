Day Twenty

The Friend of Sinners

Luke 15:1–2 & Matthew 11:19  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, the accusation meant to shame him has become one of our most beloved titles for him: friend of sinners. We confess that we sometimes prefer a Jesus who keeps a safer distance from the mess of human failure — a Jesus who is easier to explain and to present. But the Jesus of the Gospels keeps eating with the wrong people. Let us love him as he actually is, not as we would prefer him to be. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Now the tax collectors and sinners were all drawing near to hear him. And the Pharisees and the scribes grumbled, saying, 'This man receives sinners and eats with them.'"

"The Son of Man came eating and drinking, and they say, 'Look at him! A glutton and a drunkard, a friend of tax collectors and sinners!'"
Luke 15:1–2 & Matthew 11:19, ESV

The charge is meant as an insult. "A friend of tax collectors and sinners." In first-century Palestine, tax collectors were despised on two counts: they worked for the occupying Roman government, and they routinely enriched themselves by over-collecting from their own people. "Sinners" is a broader term — those who had visibly, publicly broken the law and made no effort to conceal it. To be named their friend was to be contaminated by the association. Jesus does not dispute the charge. He accepts it — and then, in perhaps the most brilliant rhetorical move in all the Gospels, he tells three stories.

Luke 15 unfolds as a direct response to the Pharisees' grumbling. The lost sheep (one gone from a hundred; a man leaves the ninety-nine to find it). The lost coin (one gone from ten; a woman sweeps the house until she finds it). The lost son (one of two; a father who has been watching the road, who runs when he sees his boy still far off). Each story has the same structure: something precious is lost, someone searches at a cost the observer thinks disproportionate, something is found, and the celebration that follows embarrasses the watching world by its intensity. The party thrown for the returned prodigal is not a small, dignified acknowledgment. It is full-throated, public, kill-the-fattened-calf rejoicing — the kind that offends everyone standing outside who thinks the celebration is undeserved.

In first-century Judaism, shared meals carried enormous social and theological weight. To eat with someone was to declare them acceptable, to include them in your circle of covenant fellowship. The Pharisees ate only with the ritually pure, because to eat with the unclean was to become, in some measure, unclean. Their concern was not petty. Holiness matters. Separation from contamination matters. The law of God says so. By the logic of their tradition, Jesus' table companions should be making him progressively less fit for the presence of God, not more.

But the logic of Jesus' table is exactly opposite — and this is the theological revolution concealed in the ordinary act of sharing food. He does not become unclean by eating with sinners. Sinners encounter something clean by eating with him. He is not a vessel that can be filled with impurity; he is a source of purification, and his source does not run dry. Light is not threatened by darkness; it illuminates it. Holiness, in Jesus, is not fragile. It is aggressive — it moves toward defilement rather than away from it, not to be consumed, but to transform.

N.T. Wright points out that Jesus' meals with sinners were not merely a social scandal. They were a enacted eschatology — a foretaste, in the present, of the great messianic banquet to come. Isaiah saw it: a feast on the mountain of the LORD for all peoples, rich food and well-aged wine (Isaiah 25:6). Jesus described it: people coming from east and west and north and south to recline at table in the kingdom of God (Luke 13:29). Every time Jesus sat down with the excluded and the broken, he was showing the world what the kingdom looks like in advance — who gets in, what the host is like, how the invitation goes out.

This is not a permissive Jesus who winks at sin. The same Jesus who ate with tax collectors told Zacchaeus's story in a way that resulted in Zacchaeus giving back fourfold what he had stolen (Luke 19:8). The same Jesus who received the woman caught in adultery looked at her and said, "Go, and sin no more" (John 8:11). His friendship is not indifference to the damage sin causes. It is a love strong enough to enter that damage and work from within it — a love that does not first require the sinner to become worthy before sitting down.

The accusation has become, two thousand years later, our comfort and our hope. He is the friend of sinners. Not the friend of those who have managed to stop sinning, or the friend of those who are making satisfying progress, or the friend of those who have earned the right to sit at the table. He is the friend of sinners — which, if we are honest about what we are, is the only title we need him to hold.

For Reflection
  1. The Pharisees practiced holiness through separation; Jesus practiced it through engagement. Which pattern is more natural to you in your own life and relationships — and what might you be missing by leaning too heavily toward one at the expense of the other?
  2. Jesus' table fellowship was a foretaste of the messianic banquet — the great feast of the kingdom (Luke 13:29; Isaiah 25:6). What does it mean to you that the kingdom is pictured not primarily as a military victory or a courtroom verdict but as a feast, with a host who is glad you came?
  3. "He receives sinners and eats with them." If this is the charge against him — how does it function as an invitation? Who do you know who might be more likely to encounter Jesus through the door of his friendship than through any other door?

Going Deeper

The significance of table fellowship in first-century Judaism cannot be overstated. Shared meals were not merely social occasions; they were statements of community, covenant, and moral belonging. The Pharisees' table restrictions were rooted in a genuine theological concern: Israel was called to be holy (set apart) from the nations, and associating with the ritually unclean was understood to compromise that holiness (Leviticus 11–15). Jesus did not reject this concern about holiness — he radicalized it in the opposite direction. His holiness was not fragile; it was aggressive and outward-moving. It engaged defilement rather than retreating from it, not to be contaminated, but to cleanse.

This is the theology of the atonement itself expressed in social form: Christ the holy one enters the domain of sin and death, not to be consumed by it, but to consume it. The Incarnation is God's willingness to eat at our table — to enter our company fully, to bear our uncleanness in his body (1 Peter 2:24), to become, as Paul puts it, "sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21), so that we might become in him what we could never become on our own. The cross is the ultimate table fellowship: the holy one sitting with sinners, not as a concession to lowered standards, but as the act by which the standards are met — in him, for us, once and finally. He still sits at table with sinners. That is what the Lord's Supper is: the ongoing meal of the one who receives sinners, eaten with those who know exactly what they are.
