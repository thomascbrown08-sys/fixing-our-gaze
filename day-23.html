Day Twenty-Three

The Compassion of the King

Matthew 9:36 & Matthew 14:14  ·  New Living Translation
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, of all the qualities ascribed to you in the Gospels, compassion appears most often as the trigger of your action. Not obligation, not strategy, not even sheer power — you moved toward people because you were moved by them. Let what moved you move us also. And let it be a corrective to the ways we have learned to look past the suffering that surrounds us without being troubled. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"When he saw the crowds, he had compassion on them because they were confused and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd."

"Jesus saw the huge crowd as he stepped from the boat, and he had compassion on them and healed their sick."
Matthew 9:36 & 14:14, NLT

The word splagchnistheis — compassion at the gut level, the visceral, wrenching kind — appears seven times in the Gospels. Five of those times it describes Jesus himself. The other two occur in parables he tells: once for the father who sees his prodigal son returning and runs to him (Luke 15:20), and once for the king who forgives a servant's enormous debt (Matthew 18:27). He chose the same word for those fictional characters that the Gospel writers reached for when they described him. The father in the parable is not incidentally compassionate — the compassion is the entire engine of the story. And so it is with Jesus.

What troubles Matthew 9:36 is not the compassion itself but what produces it. Jesus looks at the crowds — the vast, pressing, sometimes demanding, sometimes exhausting crowds of first-century Galilee — and what he sees is not inconvenience, not obligation, not the raw material of a religious movement. He sees confusion. He sees helplessness. He sees people scattered and harried and with no one reliable to lead them. Like sheep without a shepherd. The image is not flattering to human dignity, but it is profoundly accurate about human vulnerability. We are, without something larger and wiser than ourselves to orient us, genuinely lost — and Jesus sees this clearly and is moved by it rather than contemptuous of it.

Matthew 14:14 shows the same pattern in a different register. Jesus has just received word that his cousin John the Baptist has been executed by Herod. He retreats by boat to a desolate place — alone, grieving, needing space. The crowds follow him on foot, arriving before he can land. A lesser person, in a moment of personal grief, with every human justification for turning the crowds away, would have asked for more time. Instead: he had compassion on them and healed their sick. His own grief does not seal him off from theirs. His sorrow does not produce self-protection. He steps off the boat and moves toward them.

Thomas à Kempis observed that Christ was the supreme example of what it looks like to be truly for others — not performatively, not strategically, but from the center of his being. And what à Kempis saw in the Gospels is this consistent pattern: Jesus is moved by what moves those he loves. Their suffering enters him. Their confusion registers with him. He does not remain behind the glass. He is the shepherd who sees the scattered sheep not as a management problem but as a grief, and who responds accordingly.

This compassion has a direction in Matthew 9 that is worth noting. Jesus is moved by the crowds — and then, immediately (9:37–38), he tells his disciples that the harvest is plentiful and the workers are few, and instructs them to pray for more workers. Compassion without direction becomes paralysis or sentimentality. The compassion of Jesus always moves toward something: toward touch, toward healing, toward feeding, toward the sending of workers, toward the cross. His feelings are not ornamental. They drive his action. He is moved, and then he moves.

The same compassion that drove Jesus through Galilee is the compassion he brings to you today. He is not weary of you. He does not look at your confusion and helplessness with impatience or disappointment. He is the shepherd who, precisely when you feel most like a sheep without direction, is most actively present to lead. The cross — the ultimate expression of this compassion — happened because he looked at the entire human condition, saw the confusion and helplessness and lostness of it, and was moved in his center to do something about it at whatever cost to himself it required.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus saw the crowds as "confused and helpless, like sheep without a shepherd" — and was moved by that rather than contemptuous of it. How does it change your relationship with your own confusion and lostness to know that it produces compassion in Christ rather than impatience?
  2. In Matthew 14, Jesus steps off the boat into the crowd's need even in the middle of his own grief. What does this pattern suggest about the relationship between his human limitations and his divine compassion? And what does it model for us?
  3. Christ's compassion always moves toward action — it drives rather than paralyzes. Is there a person or situation before you right now toward which you feel something like compassion but have not yet moved? What would the next small step of movement look like?

Going Deeper

The image of God as shepherd is one of the most sustained metaphors in all of Scripture. It begins in the Psalms — "The LORD is my shepherd" (Psalm 23:1) — runs through Ezekiel's great indictment of Israel's false shepherds and promise of a true one (Ezekiel 34), and arrives in Jesus' own declaration: "I am the good shepherd" (John 10:11). The connection is deliberate and the implication unmistakable: the LORD who is Israel's shepherd has come in person, in Jesus, to shepherd his people directly. Ezekiel 34:23 puts it with particular clarity: "I will set up over them one shepherd, my servant David, and he shall feed them." Jesus is the fulfillment of that promise — the Davidic shepherd through whom God himself tends his scattered flock.

What is remarkable about the shepherd metaphor applied to Christ is that it holds together both his royal authority and his pastoral tenderness without tension. The same Jesus who receives universal dominion from the Ancient of Days (Daniel 7:14) is the one who searches for the single lost sheep until he finds it (Luke 15:4–5). The same one who upholds the universe by the word of his power (Hebrews 1:3) is moved in his gut by the confusion of a Galilean crowd. Kingship and compassion are not competing qualities in him — they are two faces of the same love. He is sovereign enough to rule everything and personal enough to notice the one who is lost. That combination — cosmic authority held alongside intimate, individual care — is unlike anything the world has seen in any other ruler. It is the distinctively divine shape of the good shepherd.
