Day Twenty-Seven

He Came to Seek and Save

Luke 19:5, 9–10  ·  Christian Standard Bible
Opening Prayer

Father, Zacchaeus had climbed the tree to see Jesus. He did not expect to be seen. He had made himself a spectator from a safe distance, above the crowd, hoping for a glimpse without an encounter. And Jesus looked up and found him. There are some of us who have been watching from a distance for a long time, and we are not sure we want to come down. Lord Jesus, look up. Call the name. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"When Jesus came to the place, he looked up and said to him, 'Zacchaeus, hurry and come down, because today it is necessary for me to stay at your house.'… Jesus said to him, 'Today salvation has come to this house, because he too is a son of Abraham. For the Son of Man has come to seek and to save the lost.'"
Luke 19:5, 9–10, CSB

Every detail in this story is doing work. Zacchaeus is a chief tax collector — which makes him not just disliked but categorically excluded from the synagogue community, the social fabric, the normal consolations of Jewish religious life. He is rich, which in the moral economy of first-century Palestine made people assume his wealth was built on extortion from his own people — and they were not wrong. He is short, which means when the crowd presses in to see Jesus, he cannot see over the heads in front of him. So he runs ahead and climbs a sycamore tree, positioning himself to watch without being noticed, to observe from a distance without risking engagement.

It is a portrait, in miniature, of a man who wants Jesus but on his own terms. He will come close enough to observe. He will not put himself in the position of being asked for anything. He has arranged the encounter so that he retains control of the distance.

And then Jesus stops under the tree and looks up.

The Greek word for "looked up" — anablepō — is often used elsewhere in the Gospels for acts of healing: the blind man who looked up and saw clearly (Mark 8:24–25). There is something healing in this look also. Jesus does not look up and see a tax collector and a traitor and a problem. He looks up and sees Zacchaeus — sees him by name, which means he already knows who is in the tree before he glances up. "Zacchaeus, hurry and come down, because today it is necessary for me to stay at your house." The word necessary again — the same dei we encountered in the temple at twelve years old. Not "I would like to" or "if you'll have me." A divine necessity: I must.

The crowd grumbles, as crowds often do in Luke's Gospel: "He has gone to stay with a sinful man." The observation is accurate. Jesus receives it without apology. He is at the table with Zacchaeus, and by the time the meal ends something has changed in the host. Zacchaeus stands and announces that he will give half of his goods to the poor and repay fourfold anyone he has defrauded. This is not the condition Jesus required before sitting down. It is the fruit that grows in the soil of an encounter with him. Christ did not demand repentance as the price of his presence. His presence produced the repentance.

The structure of this reversal is worth pausing over carefully, because it speaks directly to one of the most common misreadings of the gospel. Many people operate with an implicit sequence: first I change, then God accepts me. Zacchaeus's story inverts this precisely. First God comes to me — finds me where I am, not where I should be — and then the change follows as the natural consequence. This is not a license to remain as we are; Zacchaeus's transformation is genuine and costly. But it follows from the encounter rather than preceding it. The seeking is God's initiative before it is our response.

Jesus summarizes the whole pattern in one sentence: the Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost. Seek — active, initiating, moving toward rather than waiting to be found. Save — not merely forgiving what is past but restoring what is lost, recovering what was broken. And lost — the precise word for Zacchaeus's condition, for the prodigal in the far country, for the sheep that went astray. Lost is not the same as condemned. It is someone who cannot find their own way home but for whom the door is still open and the Father is still watching the road. And the Son of Man has come to bring them home himself.

For Reflection
  1. Zacchaeus wanted to see Jesus from a safe distance — close enough to observe, far enough to retain control. Is there a way you have arranged your relationship with Christ similarly? What would it look like for Jesus to call you out of the tree by name?
  2. Jesus' presence produced Zacchaeus's repentance — the change followed the encounter rather than preceded it. How does this sequence challenge the "first I fix myself, then I come to God" logic that many people operate with, perhaps including you?
  3. "The Son of Man came to seek and to save the lost" — the seeking is God's initiative. Reflect on your own story: where have you been found rather than finding? What does it mean that the initiative was his before it was yours?

Going Deeper

Jesus' declaration that "salvation has come to this house" (Luke 19:9) because Zacchaeus is "a son of Abraham" raises an important question: is this salvation based on Zacchaeus's ethnic identity, or on something else? The phrase "son of Abraham" in Luke's Gospel carries a specific theological weight beyond genealogy. In Luke 13:16, Jesus calls a bent woman "a daughter of Abraham, whom Satan bound for eighteen years" and heals her — the healing is the restoration of covenant blessing to one who belongs to the people of promise. In Galatians 3:7–9, Paul extends this concept: the true sons of Abraham are those who share his faith, not merely his bloodline. Zacchaeus is restored to the covenant community — received back into the family of those who belong to the God of Abraham — not because of his ancestry but because the seeker has been found, has received Jesus gladly, and has shown the fruit of genuine change.

The Reformed tradition speaks of salvation as encompassing more than the moment of justification — it includes regeneration (the new birth that produces new desire), justification (the legal declaration of righteousness in Christ), and sanctification (the ongoing transformation of character and life). Zacchaeus's story compresses all three into a single encounter. He is found (the seeking God's regenerating initiative), received (justification — Jesus declares salvation has come to his house), and changed (sanctification — the immediate fruit in his treatment of his wealth). The order matters: finding precedes and produces the fruit. It does not wait for the fruit before it begins.
