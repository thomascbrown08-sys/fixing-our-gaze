Day Twenty-Eight

The Great Physician

Mark 2:17  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you said those who are well have no need of a physician. We confess that we sometimes prefer to present ourselves as well — or at least not as sick as we actually are. The pride that resists diagnosis is the same pride that keeps us from the Healer. Strip it from us today. Let us come as the sick to the Physician, as those who need what only you can provide, without pretense and without delay. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And when Jesus heard it, he said to them, 'Those who are well have no need of a physician, but those who are sick. I came not to call the righteous, but sinners.'"
Mark 2:17, ESV

Jesus has just done something scandalous even by his own standards. He has not only eaten with Levi the tax collector (Mark 2:14–15); he has sat at Levi's table with a crowd of "tax collectors and sinners" so large that the Pharisees take note of it. Their question to the disciples is not a neutral inquiry: "Why does he eat with tax collectors and sinners?" It is a challenge — a statement dressed as a question. The implication: a truly holy teacher would not do this.

Jesus hears the challenge and answers it directly, with an analogy so simple it is almost disarming: physicians go to sick people. A doctor who only visited the healthy would be no doctor at all. The whole logic of medicine is need — you are called to those who require what you have, not to those who are already well. And then the application: I came not to call the righteous, but sinners.

The word "call" — kaleō — is not casual. It is used elsewhere for the divine call to salvation, the summons that goes out from God to those he is drawing to himself. He has not come to call those who have no need of what he is offering. He has come for the ones who are sick — and who, crucially, know they are sick. The Pharisees' problem is not that they are too righteous for Jesus. It is that they believe themselves to be well enough that the Physician seems unnecessary. Their self-diagnosis is the obstacle.

This is the medical logic of the gospel in miniature: the first requirement for receiving what Christ offers is an accurate account of your condition. You must know you are sick before the Physician's visit makes sense. Augustine spent the first third of his life presenting himself as essentially well — intellectually sophisticated, morally complicated, but fundamentally managing. "Our heart is restless, O Lord," he would later write, but for years he did not recognize the restlessness as a symptom of sickness; he thought it was evidence of depth. The Confessions are the story of a man slowly, painfully coming to accept an accurate diagnosis.

The Physician metaphor also carries something about the nature of the healing Christ offers. A physician does not heal by setting an example of good health for the patient to imitate. A physician heals by bringing something from outside the patient's own resources — medicine, skill, intervention — that the patient cannot supply for themselves. This is the distinction between Christianity and every other religious and moral system. Every other system is essentially a wellness program: here are the practices and disciplines and improvements by which you can make yourself better. Christ is a physician: here is what you cannot provide for yourself, offered to you as gift, doing what you could never do with your own resources.

Spurgeon observed that the hospital Christ runs has an unusual admissions policy: it only takes the most serious cases. The ones who think they might manage on their own are not yet ready to be admitted. It is the desperate who receive the most attentive care — those who arrive knowing there is nothing left to try. And the care they receive is not palliative. It is restorative. He does not merely manage the symptoms of sin; he addresses its root. He does not merely improve the patient; he makes them new.

For Reflection
  1. The Pharisees' obstacle was not excess righteousness but a flawed self-diagnosis — they believed themselves well enough not to need the Physician. In what areas of your life are you most tempted to present yourself as healthier than you actually are — to God, to others, or to yourself?
  2. Jesus says he came to "call" sinners — the same word used for the divine summons to salvation. What does it mean that the call goes out to those who need it most rather than to those who have already made themselves presentable? How does this change the way you hear that call directed at you?
  3. A physician brings resources from outside the patient's own capacity — which is why self-treatment has limits the Physician does not share. Where in your spiritual life are you still trying to heal yourself through self-improvement rather than receiving from the Physician what you cannot supply?

Going Deeper

The Physician metaphor touches on a question that has occupied Christian theology from its earliest days: the nature of sin as a condition versus sin as an act. The Augustinian tradition — which flows into the Reformed — insists that sin is fundamentally a condition before it is a behavior. We do not merely commit sinful acts; we are, by nature (apart from grace), disordered, bent inward on ourselves, unable to produce the sustained love of God and neighbor that the law requires. Augustine described this as curvatus in se — curved in on oneself. It is a disease of the will and the affections, not merely a series of bad choices.

This is why the Physician metaphor is so apt. Diseases are not solved by resolve or education or self-improvement programs, though all of these may have their place. They require something from outside the diseased system — a medicine, an intervention, a power foreign to the patient's own constitution. Calvin described the gospel as "the medicine of the soul" — not the regimen the soul must follow in order to heal itself, but the remedy applied from outside that the soul cannot generate from within. When Jesus calls sinners rather than the righteous, he is doing what physicians do: going to those who need what he has, not those who believe they already have enough. The scandal of the gospel is not its narrowness but its specific address: it is most fully received by those who are most honestly broken, and least understood by those who have managed to convince themselves they are basically fine.
