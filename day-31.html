Day Thirty-One

The Sermon Begins

Matthew 5:1–2  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, for thirty days we have watched you. We have seen your glory before time and followed you through the dust of Galilee. Now you sit down on the mountain and open your mouth — and the words that come are unlike anything the world had heard before. Prepare us to receive them not merely as ethical instruction but as the voice of the one who made us and knows what we are made for. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Seeing the crowds, he went up on the mountain, and when he sat down, his disciples came to him. And he opened his mouth and taught them."
Matthew 5:1–2, ESV

We enter Section III knowing who it is that speaks. We have spent fifteen days on his eternal nature and fifteen more following the servant through the world he made. We know where he came from before we hear what he says — which is exactly how Matthew intends us to read the Sermon on the Mount. The one who ascends this hill is not a moral philosopher proposing ethical principles to a waiting audience. He is the one through whom the world was made, now sitting down on a Galilean hillside and teaching his creation what flourishing looks like from the inside.

Matthew structures the scene with deliberate echoes of Sinai. Moses went up the mountain and came down with the law. Jesus goes up the mountain and sits down — the posture of the authoritative teacher in first-century Judaism — and speaks. The contrast Matthew is building will become explicit in the body of the Sermon: "You have heard that it was said… but I say to you." Moses received the law and transmitted it. Jesus gives it on his own authority. He is not a second Moses. He is the one Moses pointed toward.

The crowd that gathered was, by any account, a mixed assembly — the sick and the suffering of Matthew 4:24, the curious and the desperate, those who had heard something and come to see for themselves. And when Jesus sees them, Matthew says, he went up the mountain. Not away from them but up — to a place from which his voice could carry, from which the whole mixed crowd could hear. The Sermon on the Mount is not a private communication to an elite group of already-formed disciples. It is a public declaration, spoken over the heads of those who were hungry and sick and confused and seeking — addressed to his disciples but overheard by everyone.

This matters for how we read what follows. The Sermon is sometimes taken primarily as an ethical program — a higher standard than the law, a more demanding set of requirements that raises the bar for those who wish to follow Jesus. If that is its primary function, it is either an instrument of guilt (demonstrating how far short we fall) or a counsel of near-impossibility (urging us to try harder toward something we can barely approach). Neither reading does justice to the one who speaks.

The Sermon is better understood as a description of the life that grace produces — not a ladder to climb toward God's acceptance, but a portrait of the person who has been found by God and is learning to live from that finding. The Beatitudes that open it do not say "be poor in spirit and then you will qualify for the kingdom." They say "blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven." The blessing is given to the condition as it is, not to the condition as it hopes to become. Jesus is not describing the entrance requirements; he is describing the shape of the life that has already entered.

He opened his mouth and taught them. Eight words in English, and in them the most important educational event in the history of the world. The Creator of language is about to speak to the creatures he made in his image, in the language they share, about the life they were created for. There is a reason that two thousand years later, the Sermon on the Mount is still read by believers and unbelievers alike as containing something that nothing else quite captures — something about the moral grain of the universe, about what human life at its best actually looks like. The one who designed the grain is the one who describes it. Sit down. Listen. He is about to begin.

For Reflection
  1. Knowing who it is that sits down on the mountain and opens his mouth — the eternal Son, through whom all things were made — how does that change the weight you give his words? How is hearing the Sermon on the Mount different from reading a great philosopher's ethics?
  2. The Sermon was addressed to disciples but overheard by the crowds — those who were sick, confused, and seeking. Is there something about the Sermon that is particularly addressed to people who don't yet know quite what to do with Jesus? What might a seeker hear in it that a longtime believer might miss?
  3. If the Sermon describes the life that grace produces rather than the performance that earns grace, how does that change the way you read its demands? Which of its teachings do you find most difficult — and what would it mean to receive help with that, rather than straining toward it alone?

Going Deeper

The question of how to read the Sermon on the Mount has been one of the most contested in Christian history. The main interpretive positions can be sketched briefly. The first (associated with some strands of Lutheran hermeneutics) reads the Sermon primarily as law — its function is to expose the impossibility of perfect obedience and drive the hearer to grace. A second (associated with liberal Protestant ethics) reads it as a social program — an ideal for civilizational improvement. A third (associated with some Anabaptist traditions) reads it as a literal ethic for a separated community. The Reformed tradition has generally taken a fourth approach: the Sermon describes the character and conduct of the citizen of the kingdom — a real and demanding ethic, possible by grace, that describes what Spirit-produced transformation produces in a human life.

This Reformed reading does not minimize the Sermon's demands; it takes them with full seriousness as genuine descriptions of what God requires. But it locates the power for obedience not in human effort but in the grace that precedes the demand. The same Christ who sits on the mountain and describes what the kingdom citizen looks like also, by his Spirit, makes the hearer capable of becoming that person — not by erasing the struggle, but by providing a new principle of life that the unregenerate heart could not generate. The Sermon is read, in this framework, alongside the Epistles' teaching on sanctification: transformation by the Spirit, through the Word, toward the likeness of Christ. The demands are real; the resources are supplied by the one who makes them.
