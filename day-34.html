Day Thirty-Four

The Prodigal and His Father

Luke 15:20–24  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, there are two sons in this parable, and we have been both of them. We have demanded our share and spent it in the far country. We have stayed home and been resentful about it. What we have not yet fully understood is the father — who runs down the road, who throws a party, who goes out to the elder son in the field as well. Let us receive both the running and the robe today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And he arose and came to his father. But while he was still a long way off, his father saw him and felt compassion, and ran and embraced him and kissed him… But the father said to his servants, 'Bring quickly the best robe, and put it on him, and put a ring on his hand, and shoes on his feet. And bring the fattened calf and kill it, and let us eat and celebrate. For this my son was dead, and is alive again; he was lost, and is found.' And they began to celebrate."
Luke 15:20–24, ESV

This is the third of three parables in Luke 15, each one telling the same story at a different scale: something precious is lost, someone searches or waits with aching attention, the lost thing is found, and the resulting celebration seems disproportionate to everyone standing outside it. A sheep. A coin. A son. The scale escalates. The celebration escalates. And the one who receives the news of the finding and does not celebrate — the Pharisee, the grumbling elder brother — grows progressively more central as the target of the parable's sharpest edge.

But today we linger on the father.

He has been watching the road. This is the detail that tends to pass unmarked, but it is everything. A father who had decided his son was gone would eventually stop watching the road — would turn back to the work of the farm, the management of the remaining son, the life that continues despite loss. This father has not turned away. He is still watching. Which means the sight of his son "still a long way off" is the sight of a man who has been looking in exactly that direction, in exactly that posture of waiting, long enough to recognize a silhouette at maximum distance.

And then he does something that no dignified father in the ancient Near East did: he runs. In first-century culture, an older man running was undignified — it required gathering up the robes, exposing the legs, abandoning the gravity that age and status conferred. He does not wait for his son to complete the long walk of shame. He abandons his dignity to close the distance faster. He is running not to prevent the son from finishing his prepared speech but because he cannot wait another moment. The speech comes anyway — "Father, I have sinned against heaven and before you; I am no longer worthy to be called your son" — and the father talks over it. He is not interested in the son's renegotiation of the terms. He is giving his son back his identity before the son has finished asking for a servant's position.

The best robe. A ring. Shoes. The fattened calf. Each item is a restoration signal in the economy of the story. The robe covers the rags of the far country. The ring restores family authority. The shoes distinguish him from the servants who go barefoot — he is not hired help; he is a son. And the feast is not a quiet, private welcome-home but a public celebration — the whole household, the neighbors, the noise of music reaching as far as the fields where the elder brother is still at work.

There is a strand of theology — and it is a correct one — that reads this parable primarily as a story about repentance. The son "came to himself" (Luke 15:17), arose, rehearsed his confession, went home. All of this is real and matters. But the weight of the parable falls not on the son's return but on the father's response to it. The son came back. The father ran. The movement that defines the story is the father's movement toward the son — the one who was watching the road, who saw the silhouette, who ran, who covered the distance, who covered the shame with the best robe. The repentance was real; the grace was prior, larger, and more eager than the repentance deserved.

Henri Nouwen, in his meditation on this parable, observed that the father's embrace is the embrace of one who has been waiting to give it — who has, in a sense, been practicing the embrace the whole time the son was gone. There is no hesitation in it, no holding back to see if the son means it, no probationary period before the full welcome. The welcome is total and immediate. The party starts the same night. And Jesus tells this story to a crowd that includes Pharisees who think sinners should be required to earn their way back to the table — and who are about to watch Jesus go and have dinner with them anyway.

For Reflection
  1. The father was watching the road while the son was still far off — which means he never stopped hoping. What does this sustained, active, watchful love tell you about the posture of the Father toward you in your own moments of wandering or distance?
  2. The father ran to close the distance — abandoning dignity to reach his son faster. What does this running say about the character of God toward those who have been lost in the far country? Does this portrait of God match the one you carry inside?
  3. The elder brother's problem is not absence but resentment — he was home but not inside the party. He had never left, but he had also never really received what the household freely offered. Which son do you most identify with right now — and what does the father's response to each of them say to you?

Going Deeper

The parable of the prodigal son is sometimes called the parable of the two lost sons — and the description is more accurate. Both sons are estranged from the father, but in opposite directions. The younger son's estrangement is obvious: he takes his inheritance early (a culturally scandalous act, equivalent to wishing his father dead), wastes it, and ends up feeding pigs in a Gentile region — the furthest possible point from Jewish covenant identity. The elder son's estrangement is subtler and, in some ways, more alarming: he has been present in the household the whole time, obedient by external measure, but relates to his father as an employer rather than a parent — "Look, these many years I have served you… yet you never gave me a young goat" (Luke 15:29). He thinks of himself as a worker earning wages, not a son receiving an inheritance. He has never left, but he has also never received.

Tim Keller's insight here is characteristically sharp: both sons want the father's things rather than the father himself. The younger takes his share and leaves. The elder earns his share through performance. Neither has yet understood that the father's gift is not primarily the inheritance but himself — his presence, his fellowship, his love. The gospel addressed to the younger son is the gospel of the returning prodigal: you can come home. The gospel addressed to the elder son is, if anything, more challenging: you have been home all along and have not yet understood that this is home. Come inside. Stop performing for what is already freely yours. The fattened calf is not the reward for your years of service. It is the expression of a love that has been waiting all along to give itself fully to you — if only you will receive it rather than earn it.
