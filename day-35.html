Day Thirty-Five

The Good Samaritan

Luke 10:33–37  ·  New Living Translation
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the lawyer wanted to justify himself — to draw the boundary of "neighbor" tightly enough that his current practice would qualify. You told him a story that blew the boundary wide open and then asked him which figure in the story had been a neighbor. He could not avoid the answer. Give us today the same unavoidable clarity about what loving our neighbors actually requires, and the grace to stop looking for reasons to narrow the definition. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Then a despised Samaritan came along, and when he saw the man, he felt compassion for him. Going over to him, the Samaritan soothed his wounds with olive oil and wine and bandaged them. Then he put the man on his own donkey and took him to an inn, where he took care of him. The next day he handed the innkeeper two silver coins… 'Take care of this man. If his bill runs higher than this, I'll pay you the next time I'm here.' Now which of these three would you say was a neighbor to the man who was attacked by bandits?"
Luke 10:33–36, NLT

A lawyer has asked Jesus a question designed to establish his own righteousness: "Who is my neighbor?" The question assumes that "neighbor" is a category with knowable boundaries — that if the definition can be settled, one's obligations can be calculated and discharged. Jesus does not answer the question as asked. He tells a story that reframes the question entirely — and at its center places a character whose presence in the role of hero would have struck his first audience as almost offensive.

The road from Jerusalem to Jericho descended roughly 3,300 feet in seventeen miles through rocky, isolated terrain — notorious for bandits. The priest who sees the beaten man and "passed by on the other side" may have had reasons his tradition would have acknowledged: contact with a potentially dead body would render him ritually unclean and unfit for temple service. The Levite, similarly. We should not read these figures as simply heartless; they were navigating genuine competing obligations within their religious framework. Their failure is not cruelty — it is the failure of a system of obligations that has become more concerned with its own preservation than with the human being bleeding in the ditch.

The Samaritan is the shock. Jews and Samaritans shared centuries of mutual contempt — religious, ethnic, and political. The Samaritan had every culturally available reason to pass by. Instead: he saw the man, felt compassion, went over, soothed the wounds with oil and wine, bandaged them, put him on his own donkey, took him to an inn, stayed the night caring for him, paid for his continued care, and promised to cover any additional costs on his return. The parable does not specify that he was moved by the man's Jewish identity. He was moved by the man's need. And he did not stop until the need was met — not merely addressed, but fully covered, including costs he had not yet incurred.

The parable has always been read as a call to love those outside our natural circles of solidarity — those whom culture or religion or politics has sorted into the category of "other." And rightly so. But there is a deeper reading that the church fathers pressed, and that the whole context of Luke 10 supports. The lawyer has asked, "What must I do to inherit eternal life?" and Jesus has directed him to the love command. The parable then shows what that love looks like in practice — and the conclusion is devastating: "Go and do likewise." No one reading this story with even moderate honesty can say that the Samaritan's response is the ordinary standard of their own life. The parable's function is not primarily to give us a warm model to admire. It is to make us feel how far short we fall — and thus to make us desperate for the one who does not fall short, whose love for those in the ditch (including us) was costly, sustained, and total.

Because there is one who saw us stripped and wounded by the roadside, who was not obligated by any natural solidarity to help us, who was moved by compassion, who bound up our wounds at his own expense, who gave over everything required to secure our restoration, and who promised to return. The Samaritan of the parable is also — the church fathers were right to notice — a mirror of the one who told the story. He is the neighbor, before he is the example of neighboring.

For Reflection
  1. The lawyer wanted to define "neighbor" narrowly enough to justify his current practice. Where do you find yourself drawing the boundary of neighbor tightly — by geography, by similarity, by social group, by what you can reasonably be expected to manage? What would the Samaritan's logic do to that boundary?
  2. The priest and Levite were not simply heartless — they were navigating real competing obligations. When do your own legitimate obligations or sensible cautions function as a way to avoid a neighbor's need rather than as a genuine constraint? How do you tell the difference?
  3. If Christ is the ultimate Good Samaritan — the one who bound up our wounds at his own great cost — how does receiving that care change the way you extend it to others? Does knowing you have been the one in the ditch make you more or less willing to stop for someone else?

Going Deeper

The allegorical reading of the Good Samaritan — in which the wounded man is humanity, the Samaritan is Christ, the inn is the church, and the innkeeper is the ministers of the gospel — was widespread in the patristic tradition (Origen, Augustine, and others developed it at length) and was never without its defenders in the Reformed tradition, though Calvin himself was cautious about allegorical excess. The parable's primary function as an ethical instruction about love of neighbor is not negated by the allegorical reading — the two operate simultaneously. Jesus tells the story both to expose the inadequacy of the lawyer's love and to show the shape of his own love for the wounded.

The deeper question the parable presses is the one the lawyer was trying to evade: not "who is my neighbor?" (a boundary-drawing question) but "to whom am I being a neighbor?" (a question about action). Jesus reframes the lawyer's question from passive definition to active engagement. The answer to "who is my neighbor?" is: whoever is in front of you in need. The answer to "what must I do?" is: stop, get involved, bear the cost, stay until the care is complete. These are not manageable obligations that can be calculated and discharged. They are expressions of a love that has no natural ceiling — which is why, in the end, they point beyond themselves to the one whose love for the man in the ditch was without limit. Only from having received that love, in its full uncalculated extravagance, can we begin to extend what we ourselves could not generate.
