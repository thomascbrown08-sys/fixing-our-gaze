Day Thirty-Seven

The Pearl of Great Price

Matthew 13:45–46  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, the merchant in this parable sells everything he has. Not most of it. Not what he can afford to lose. Everything. And he does it with apparent joy — because what he has found is worth more than everything else combined. Let that calculus sink into us today: not as a demand to divest ourselves of joy, but as the natural response of someone who has seen what the pearl actually is. We have not yet seen it fully enough to sell everything gladly. Help us to see it. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Again, the kingdom of heaven is like a merchant in search of fine pearls, who, on finding one pearl of great value, went and sold all that he had and bought it."
Matthew 13:45–46, ESV

Two sentences. One of the shortest parables in all of the Gospels. Its brevity is part of its power: there is nothing to look at except the action. A merchant finds a pearl of extraordinary value, and he sells everything he has to buy it. That is the whole story. Jesus does not explain why the merchant does it, or what the pearl looks like, or what emotions attend the selling. The parable is simply the bare structure of the event — and the bare structure says everything.

The merchant is a professional. He is in "search of fine pearls" — this is not a casual shopper who stumbles onto something unexpected. He knows what he is looking for, he knows quality when he sees it, and he has probably been looking for a long time. When he finds this pearl, his trained eye recognizes immediately that what he is looking at surpasses everything in his inventory and everything he has encountered in a career of searching. The recognition is not wishful thinking. It is professional judgment: this is worth everything.

And so he sells all that he has. The parable does not soften this. It is not "he liquidated his less profitable holdings" or "he reinvested his discretionary assets." All. His other pearls, his business capital, presumably his house and transport and livelihood — everything converts into the purchase price. And this is presented not as tragedy but as obvious good sense. Given the value of the pearl, the exchange is rational. You only lose when you give up something worth more than what you receive. The merchant is not impoverished by the transaction; he is enriched, because what he now holds outvalues everything he gave to obtain it.

The parable sits in a cluster of kingdom parables in Matthew 13, and it corresponds to the parable immediately before it — the hidden treasure in the field (Matthew 13:44) — in which a man discovers something of immense value and also sells all he has for joy. Both parables make the same point at different angles: the kingdom is worth everything. Not worth quite a lot. Not worth significant sacrifice. Worth everything you have and more.

C.S. Lewis put the principle behind this parable in characteristically lucid terms: "It would seem that Our Lord finds our desires not too strong, but too weak. We are half-hearted creatures, fooling about with drink and sex and ambition when infinite joy is offered us, like an ignorant child who wants to go on making mud pies in a slum because he cannot imagine what is meant by the offer of a holiday at the sea." The tragedy is not that we want too much; it is that we want too little. We have settled for pearls of modest value — security, approval, comfort, a manageable life — when the one pearl worth having is on offer.

Jonathan Edwards described what he called "the beauty of Christ" — the attractiveness, the desirability, the loveliness of the Person of Jesus himself — as the governing principle of Christian affection. We do not obey Christ reluctantly, from mere duty. We sell all that we have for him because when we have actually seen him — really seen him, not just heard about him — the calculus changes. The mud pies lose their appeal. The problem is usually not that we have thought about Christ and decided he is not worth everything. The problem is that we have not yet seen him clearly enough for the "selling all" to feel like the obvious, joyful thing it is for the merchant in the story.

That is the prayer these ninety days are built around. Look. Look longer. Look more carefully. The pearl is worth more than you have yet understood. And the one who found him and sold all reported: the joy of finding justified the cost of giving. Always.

For Reflection
  1. The merchant sells all that he has — not reluctantly but as an obviously rational response to what he has found. Are there things in your life that you are holding onto that function as alternative pearls — things you have not yet been willing to subordinate to Christ? What would need to change about how you see Christ for the releasing to feel natural rather than costly?
  2. C.S. Lewis says our problem is that our desires are too weak, not too strong — that we settle for lesser things because we cannot yet imagine what the greater thing is. What lesser things are you most tempted to settle for? And what do you think has limited your vision of the greater?
  3. The merchant's joy at finding the pearl preceded the selling — the joy of finding motivated the selling rather than following it. What does this say about the relationship between joy in Christ and the sacrifices of following him? Does this sequence match your experience?

Going Deeper

The parable of the pearl of great price is one of the primary texts in the New Testament for what the Reformed tradition, following Edwards, calls the affectional dimension of saving faith — the idea that genuine faith is not merely intellectual assent to propositions about Christ but includes the engagement of the will and the affections. The merchant does not merely believe the pearl is valuable; he desires it enough to act. And the action is total, not partial.

Edwards' great work Religious Affections argues at length that the distinguishing mark of genuine Christianity is precisely the reordering of what the heart prizes most — what it loves, fears, hopes in, and delights in. This is not emotionalism; it is the claim that genuine transformation, by the Spirit, reaches the level of what we want, not only what we believe or do. The person in whom the Spirit has genuinely worked does not ultimately cling to the mud pies in preference to the sea. The affections are renewed; new desires emerge; the pearl becomes genuinely desirable above all competitors. The Chalmers principle — the expulsive power of a new affection — is exactly this: you do not stop loving lesser things by effort, but by finding something worth loving more. The merchant did not need to be commanded to sell his other pearls. He was glad to. That gladness is the mark that the finding was genuine, and that the one found is really the pearl of great price.
