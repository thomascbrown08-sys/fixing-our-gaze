Day Forty-One

The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

Luke 18:10–14  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, this parable has a villain and a hero, and every reader instinctively identifies with the hero. That may be the first problem. The Pharisee did not go to the temple intending to be proud; he went to give God thanks. The tax collector did not go intending to be righteous; he went because he had nowhere else to go. Give us honesty today about which prayer is actually ours — and mercy enough to pray the one that went home justified. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"The Pharisee, standing, prayed thus with himself: 'God, I thank you that I am not like other men, extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even like this tax collector. I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I get.' But the tax collector, standing far off, would not even lift up his eyes to heaven, but beat his chest, saying, 'God, be merciful to me, a sinner!' I tell you, this man went down to his house justified, rather than the other."
Luke 18:11–14, ESV

Luke introduces this parable with an unusual editorial note — unusual because Jesus rarely required his parables to be labeled. "He also told this parable to some who trusted in themselves that they were righteous, and treated others with contempt" (Luke 18:9). The audience is specified. This parable is not for everyone equally; it is directed at a particular spiritual condition. And the reader who feels certain the condition is not theirs is probably the reader who most needs to keep reading.

The Pharisee is not a hypocrite in the common sense. He is not pretending to virtues he does not possess. He fasts twice a week — the law required only once a year on the Day of Atonement; he is doing five times what is required. He tithes on everything — many people tithed only on the major agricultural products; he tithes all. By any objective external measure, his religious practice is exemplary. And his prayer of thanksgiving is offered to God. The problem is not that he is ungrateful. The problem is what he is grateful for: that he is better than others.

The prayer is technically addressed to God but functionally directed at the tax collector standing nearby. "God, I thank you that I am not like other men… or even like this tax collector." The comparison is the center of gravity. His self-assessment requires the other man as its reference point. He does not know himself before God; he knows himself against his neighbor. And this is the rotten root: his righteousness is relational in the wrong direction — it is calibrated by the distance between himself and the worst people available, rather than by the distance between himself and the holy God before whom he stands.

The tax collector has no such comparison available to him. Or rather, he does not reach for one. He stands far off — the posture of someone who is not sure he belongs in the space at all — and he cannot lift his eyes. His prayer is barely a sentence: God, be merciful to me, a sinner. The Greek behind "be merciful" is hilaskomai — a sacrificial term, invoking the mercy seat, the place of atonement in the temple. He is not asking for mild patience. He is asking for the whole weight of divine mercy to fall on his condition. And he identifies himself simply, without comparison or qualification: a sinner. Not the worst sinner — he makes no claims about others. Just: a sinner. That is all he has to offer and all he offers.

Jesus' verdict lands without hesitation: this man went home justified. Not the other. The man who came with everything went away empty. The man who came with nothing went away full. The inversion is total and deliberate — it is the first-and-last logic of the kingdom in its starkest form.

Tim Keller has pressed on the subtle but important point that the danger the Pharisee represents is not limited to obviously religious people. The same structure — measuring yourself favorably against others to establish your own standing — can operate in entirely secular forms. The progressive who judges the bigot, the intellectual who judges the anti-intellectual, the moral person who thanks their lucky stars they are not like those people over there: the prayer changes content but not shape. The question this parable asks is not "are you religious?" but "where are you standing when you pray?" Far off, with nothing to offer but your need? Or close, with a ledger full of comparative merit?

For Reflection
  1. The Pharisee's righteousness was calibrated against his neighbors rather than against God. Where do you find yourself doing the same — measuring your standing by the distance between yourself and someone worse, rather than by the standard of holiness before which all comparisons dissolve?
  2. "God, be merciful to me, a sinner" — five words without elaboration, comparison, or qualification. When did you last pray something this simple? What would it cost you to make this prayer genuinely your own rather than a formula you assent to?
  3. Luke says Jesus told this parable to those "who trusted in themselves that they were righteous and treated others with contempt." Is there any way in which your trust in your own righteousness has produced contempt — even mild, even well-hidden — for others? What does this parable do to that?

Going Deeper

The word "justified" in Luke 18:14 — dedikaiōmenos — is the same word Paul deploys in his great exposition of justification by faith in Romans and Galatians. To be justified is to be declared righteous before God, acquitted, the verdict rendered in one's favor. Luke uses it here in a way that is entirely consistent with Paul's later theology: the one declared righteous is the one who came with no righteousness of his own to offer, who asked for mercy rather than recognition. The Pharisee brought his righteousness and it was not accepted. The tax collector brought his poverty and it was met with mercy.

This is the doctrine of justification by faith alone in narrative form. The tax collector is not justified because his prayer was better-crafted or more sincere than the Pharisee's — he is justified because he cast himself entirely on divine mercy rather than on divine assessment of his merit. Luther called this the simul justus et peccator — simultaneously righteous and sinner — meaning that the Christian's standing before God is always and only grounded in Christ's righteousness received by faith, never in a personal righteousness that has been achieved or accumulated. The moment you reach for the comparative — "at least I am not like…" — you have stepped out of the tax collector's posture and into the Pharisee's. The prayer that goes home justified is always the same prayer: God, be merciful to me, a sinner. Not once, at conversion, but every day, for the whole of the Christian life.
