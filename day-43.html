Day Forty-Three

The Narrow Door

Luke 13:23–27  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, this passage is among your most uncomfortable. It speaks of urgency, of doors that close, of people who assumed their proximity to you entitled them to entry and found themselves outside. We do not want to minimize it or resolve it too quickly. Let it land with its full weight, and then let us ask honestly: have we passed through the narrow door, or are we among those who eat and drink in your presence and yet do not truly know you? Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Strive to enter through the narrow door. For many, I tell you, will seek to enter and will not be able. When once the master of the house has risen and shut the door, and you begin to stand outside and to knock at the door, saying, 'Lord, open to us,' then he will answer you, 'I do not know where you come from.'… And you will begin to say, 'We ate and drank in your presence, and you taught in our streets.' But he will say, 'I tell you, I do not know where you come from.'"
Luke 13:24–27, ESV

Someone asks Jesus a question that sounds theological but is really a way of avoiding the personal: "Lord, will those who are saved be few?" It is a question about others — about the salvation rate of humanity in general, a kind of census inquiry. Jesus refuses to answer it. He answers a different question, the one the questioner should have asked: Strive to enter through the narrow door. Never mind the others. What about you?

The word translated "strive" — agōnizomai — is an athletic term, the root of our word "agony." It suggests intensive, determined effort, the kind of exertion that is willing to be uncomfortable. This is not a counsel of salvation by works — the door is narrow, which means the entry cannot be earned by fitting the right credentials through it. The striving is the effort of serious, urgent attention: do not drift, do not assume, do not treat the door as something you will walk through whenever it suits you. The door requires that you actually go through it, with your whole self, and not as a theoretical exercise.

The urgency of the image is enforced by what follows. The master rises and shuts the door. It closes. And those standing outside who knock and say "Lord, open to us" receive a devastating response: I do not know where you come from. The horror is not that these people were strangers to Jesus. They ate and drank in his presence. He taught in their streets. They had religious proximity, cultural familiarity, doctrinal awareness — all the markers of belonging to the community around him. And yet he says he does not know them. Not "I know you but cannot let you in." I do not know you.

Jesus draws the most sobering possible distinction between proximity and relationship, between being near to him and being known by him. There are people who are familiar with Jesus in every cultural and intellectual sense — who can quote him, who attend churches that teach about him, who have heard the gospel many times — who are not, in the sense that matters most, known by him. The door requires not merely acquaintance but entering: not merely hearing but receiving, not merely being near but being in.

This passage is one of the hardest in the Gospels for those who prefer a comfortable, non-urgent Christianity. It does not permit the assumption that exposure equals membership, that proximity equals relationship, that cultural Christianity is the same as the thing itself. Jesus is not harsh here — he is honest. And his honesty is itself an act of love: he is warning, in the present tense, people who still have time to go through the door. The door has not yet closed. The master has not yet risen. The warning is still an invitation.

What does striving to enter the narrow door look like? It does not look like performing harder or accumulating more religious merit. It looks like the tax collector at the back of the temple: coming through the door of your own poverty, your own need, your own honest acknowledgment that you have no standing of your own — and finding that the narrowness of the door is precisely fitted to someone who enters without baggage, without comparison, without claims. The door is narrow not to exclude the undeserving but to exclude the self-sufficient. Those who come empty go through easily. Those who come loaded with their credentials find the door too small to fit them through.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus refused to answer the question about how many will be saved and redirected it to personal urgency: what about you? Is there a way you use general theological questions about salvation — who will be saved, how exclusive is Christianity — as a way of avoiding the personal question? What would it look like to take the personal question seriously?
  2. "I do not know where you come from" — spoken to people who had eaten and drunk in Jesus' presence. What is the difference between proximity to Jesus and being known by him? By that measure, where do you stand?
  3. The door is narrow but it is still open. The warning is still an invitation. If the urgency of this passage lands in you at all — if there is a question mark about your own standing — what would it mean to go through the door today, rather than deferring it?

Going Deeper

The narrow door passage is one of several in the Gospels where Jesus speaks with what can only be called eschatological urgency — a pressing of the present moment with the weight of ultimate consequences. The Reformed tradition has always been willing to preach both the free offer of the gospel (the door is open; all are invited; come now) and the genuine danger of delay (the door closes; there is a time when it will be too late). Both truths are present in the text, and both must be preached without flattening either. The free offer without urgency produces complacency. The urgency without the free offer produces despair. Together, they produce the response Jesus is calling for: serious, attentive, unhesitating movement through the door that is still open.

The statement "I do not know you" has a precise parallel in Matthew 7:23, where it appears at the end of the Sermon on the Mount: "I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness." In both passages, the people who hear this verdict had some form of engagement with Jesus that fell short of genuine relationship. Matthew 7 specifies that they even prophesied and cast out demons in his name — remarkable spiritual activities, outwardly impressive, apparently authorized. And yet "I never knew you." The knowledge Jesus speaks of is not cognitive recognition but the intimate, personal knowledge of a relationship entered and sustained — the knowledge of a sheep who knows the shepherd's voice (John 10:3–4). This is the knowledge that the narrow door admits: not the knowledge of those who have heard about him, but of those who have heard him, and followed, and been known.
