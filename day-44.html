Day Forty-Four

The Rich Young Ruler

Mark 10:17–22  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, he had so much going for him. He was young, wealthy, morally serious, and genuinely eager. He ran to you. He knelt. He asked the right question. And then you looked at him and loved him and named the one thing he was not willing to do — and he went away sad. We confess that we have our own one things. Give us grace today not to walk away from the love that sees them clearly. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And Jesus, looking at him, loved him, and said to him, 'You lack one thing: go, sell all that you have and give to the poor, and you will have treasure in heaven; and come, follow me.' Disheartened by the saying, he went away sorrowful, for he had great possessions."
Mark 10:21–22, ESV

He came running and knelt before Jesus — a posture of urgency and deference that was unusual for a man of his standing. His question is direct and serious: "Good Teacher, what must I do to inherit eternal life?" He is not testing Jesus. He is genuinely seeking. He has kept the commandments from his youth, he says, and the text gives no indication that this is a lie. He is, by every visible measure, an excellent candidate for the kingdom. Young. Wealthy. Morally earnest. Enthusiastic.

And then Mark records the most important sentence in the encounter: Jesus, looking at him, loved him. This is the only place in the Synoptic Gospels where we are told explicitly that Jesus loved a particular named person in the moment of an encounter. It is placed here, before the hardest thing Jesus will say to him, as the motivation for the hardness. What follows is not a test of willingness or a display of authority. It is the act of a person who loves this man enough to tell him the truth about what is holding him back.

One thing you lack. Not many things — one. And it is not any of the commandments he has kept. It is the thing behind the commandments: the one loyalty that has never been surrendered, the one security that has never been placed on the altar, the one competitor for the throne of his heart that he has never been willing to dethrone. Go, sell all that you have, give it to the poor, and come, follow me.

The instruction is specific to this man. Jesus is not issuing a universal command to everyone that they must sell all possessions to follow him — Zacchaeus gave half his goods and was received, not corrected. Jesus is performing a diagnostic. He has looked at this man with loving, clear eyes and he knows exactly what the idol is. He names it. He offers the trade: give up the one thing, and receive everything. Come, follow me.

The man went away sorrowful. This is among the most heartbreaking moments in the Gospels. He does not argue. He does not bargain. He does not misunderstand the instruction. He understands perfectly, which is why his face falls and why he leaves. He wanted eternal life, but he was not willing to let go of the one thing standing between him and it. And it cost him everything to hold onto the one thing — because the one thing he kept was not life. Jesus was life, and he walked away from him.

The disciples are astonished — they ask who then can be saved, because if this man cannot, there is little hope for anyone. And Jesus answers in a way that reframes everything: "With man it is impossible, but not with God. For all things are possible with God" (Mark 10:27). The door the rich young man could not open is opened from the other side. What no one can do through sheer willpower — giving up the idol, turning away from the competitor — God can accomplish in a heart that is willing to be worked on. The tragedy of this encounter is not that the door was closed. It is that he closed it himself, and walked away while it was still open.

Jesus watched him go. The text does not say he called after him. He let him go — because love that is real does not override the will, even when it is breaking to watch the will choose wrongly. But the love that watched him walk away is the same love that is watching you now. And it still knows the one thing.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus looked at the man and loved him before he spoke the hard word. The hard word came from the love, not instead of it. Is there a hard word you have been resisting — from Scripture, from the Spirit, from a trusted friend — that might actually be love looking at you clearly? What makes it hard to receive?
  2. "One thing you lack" — Jesus identified not a list of failings but the single specific thing that was functioning as the true competitor for the man's heart. What is the one thing in your life that most competes with Christ for first place? You probably already know what it is.
  3. The man went away sorrowful but not yet beyond reach — the text does not say he never returned. Is there a way in which you have walked away from something Christ asked of you and have been carrying the sorrow of that choice? What would it mean to turn back?

Going Deeper

The encounter with the rich young ruler has been a touchstone for discussions of the relationship between wealth, discipleship, and salvation across the whole history of the church. Some patristic writers (particularly Clement of Alexandria) spiritualized the command — arguing that "selling all" meant interior detachment rather than literal divestiture. The Reformers generally resisted this spiritualization while insisting that the command was specific to this man's diagnostic situation rather than a universal requirement of poverty for all believers. Calvin's comment is careful: the instruction reveals the principle — that nothing may have a stronger hold on our hearts than Christ — while the specific form (selling possessions) was calibrated to this particular man's particular idol.

What is universally applicable is the diagnostic principle: there is, for every person, a "one thing" — a loyalty, a security, a comfort, a status — that functions as the real competitor for the heart's throne. The idol is not always money. It may be reputation, or a relationship, or an achievement, or an identity. But it is always something that, when Jesus touches it, produces the same expression that fell across this man's face: disheartened, going away sorrowful. The test of what our idol is, is precisely what the rich young ruler's test revealed: what we cannot give up when Christ asks for it is the thing that has us. And the gospel's answer is not merely "try harder to give it up" but "behold something worth more — and find that the giving up comes in the wake of the beholding." This is the expulsive power of a new affection: not white-knuckling the release, but being given a greater love that makes the old one possible to let go.
