Day Forty-Seven

Peace, Be Still

Mark 4:37–41  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, you were asleep in the boat — genuinely, humanly asleep, exhausted from a day of teaching — when the storm came. The disciples woke you in a panic, and you stood up and spoke to the wind and the water as though they were servants awaiting instructions. Let the sheer strangeness of this land on us today. And let the question the disciples asked in the aftermath be our question too: Who then is this? Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And a great windstorm arose, and the waves were breaking into the boat, so that the boat was already filling. But he was in the stern, asleep on the cushion. And they woke him and said to him, 'Teacher, do you not care that we are perishing?' And he awoke and rebuked the wind and said to the sea, 'Peace! Be still!' And the wind ceased, and there was a great calm. He said to them, 'Why are you so afraid? Have you still no faith?' And they were filled with great fear and said to one another, 'Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?'"
Mark 4:37–41, ESV

Two things happen in this passage, and they must both be held. First: Jesus is asleep. Genuinely, deeply asleep — on a cushion, in the stern, through a storm violent enough that professional fishermen are terrified. This is exhaustion. This is a human body at its limit after a long day. The Son of God, through whom the seas were made, is sleeping inside one of them. His humanity is real and specific and evidenced in the unremarkable physiology of fatigue.

Second: he wakes up and speaks to the storm, and it obeys him immediately and completely. Not gradually — not the way storms normally abate, with the wind dying down over several minutes and the waves settling slowly. Immediately there was a great calm. He rebukes the wind the way he rebukes unclean spirits — with a word of command — and the wind responds the way the spirits respond: instantly, without negotiation, because it has no choice.

The disciples' panic had taken the form of an accusation: "Do you not care that we are perishing?" It is a question born of genuine fear and genuine feeling, and it is also subtly wrong. He was asleep, which they interpreted as indifference. But sleep is not indifference — it is rest. The disciples could not rest because the storm terrified them. Jesus could rest because even asleep, he was the one whose authority extended to the waves. His rest was not inattention to the danger; it was the rest of the one who knew exactly how much the danger was capable of and who held the limit.

Psalm 89:9 declares of YHWH: "You rule the raging of the sea; when its waves rise, you still them." Psalm 107:29 says God "made the storm be still, and the waves of the sea were hushed." When Jesus speaks to the storm in Mark 4, the crowd does not simply see a miracle. They see YHWH acting — the God of Israel, the one who parted the Red Sea and stilled the waters at Jericho, doing what only he can do. In a boat. After a nap. The disciples' response is instructive: they were "filled with great fear" — not the ordinary fear of the storm, but the awe-fear that comes from standing in the presence of something too large to contain. The storm had frightened them. What he did with the storm terrified them differently, more deeply, because it pointed to something more fundamental about who was in the boat with them.

And their question — Who then is this? — is the right question. It is the question this whole devotional is built around, the question that every encounter with Jesus ultimately presses on the observer. The storm was a classroom, and the lesson was not about meteorology. It was about Christology. The disciples had spent months watching him. And in the calm that followed the storm, they looked at each other and realized they were still only beginning to understand who they were following.

You are in the boat too. The storm is real — whatever its particular shape in your life. And he is present, perhaps in a manner that looks like inattention. The question is whether the same faith that should have kept the disciples from panic is available to you: not the faith that produces immunity from storms, but the faith that rests in the presence of the one who holds the limit of every storm that has ever risen against those he loves.

For Reflection
  1. The disciples interpreted Jesus' sleep as indifference: "Do you not care?" Have you ever interpreted God's apparent silence or inactivity during a crisis as indifference? What does the stilling of the storm say about the relationship between God's rest and God's care?
  2. Jesus' authority extends to the created order — wind, water, the physical world — as well as to disease, demons, and sin. What storm in your present life most needs to hear the word "Peace, be still"? What would it mean to bring it specifically to the one who spoke those words?
  3. "Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?" The disciples had been with him for months and were still asking this question. Has your answer to this question grown or deepened over the past forty-seven days of fixing your gaze on him? What do you now see that you did not see before?

Going Deeper

The stilling of the storm is one of the miracles that most clearly demonstrates what the New Testament theologians call the "nature miracles" of Jesus — those that show his authority over the physical creation rather than over individual human bodies or spiritual forces. The theological significance is precise: the one who made the natural order retains authority over it. The wind and sea are not autonomous systems running on their own momentum; they are creatures, sustained and governed by the word of the one who made them (Hebrews 1:3; Colossians 1:17). When Jesus speaks to the storm, he is not overriding the natural order from the outside — he is reasserting the authority of the one who maintains the natural order from within it, as its source.

The two fears in this passage are worth distinguishing theologically. The disciples' fear of the storm is the ordinary fear of creatures before a power that threatens them. Their fear after the stilling is the fear of the Lord — what the Old Testament calls yir'at YHWH, the reverential awe that belongs to creatures in the presence of their Creator. It is not terror in the sense of wanting to flee; it is the trembling that comes from suddenly perceiving the actual scale of what is present. The psalmists speak of this fear repeatedly as the beginning of wisdom (Psalm 111:10) — not the fear that drives us away from God but the fear that drives us to our knees before him. The disciples end the storm story in exactly the right posture: not reassured that everything will be fine, but undone by the knowledge of who is in the boat. Both the reassurance and the undoing are available to those who bring their storms to him.
