Day Forty-Eight

Who Then Is This?

John 10:37–38 & Mark 4:41  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, you invited people to look at your works as the evidence of your identity — not to argue about metaphysics but to see what you were doing and reason from there to who you are. Today we pause not on a specific miracle or teaching but on the question itself: Who are you? We have been asking it for forty-eight days. We are still asking it. And we find that the asking does not exhaust the answer. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"If I am not doing the works of my Father, then do not believe me; but if I do them, even though you do not believe me, believe the works, that you may know and understand that the Father is in me and I am in the Father."

"Who then is this, that even the wind and the sea obey him?"
John 10:37–38 & Mark 4:41, ESV

Yesterday's question from the disciples in the boat — Who then is this? — is the central question of the entire Gospel of Mark, and of the New Testament as a whole. It is also, in a very real sense, the central question of human history. Every other question about meaning, purpose, ethics, ultimate hope, and the nature of reality resolves differently depending on how you answer this one. So today we pause and sit with the question directly, using two texts that illuminate it from different angles.

In John 10:37–38, Jesus himself provides an epistemological method for answering it. He is speaking to those who remain unconvinced and perhaps hostile. And he says something remarkable: he does not ask them to start with his identity claims and work toward belief. He invites them to start with his works — to look at what he is doing — and reason from the works toward the identity. If the works are the works of God, then the one doing them stands in a unique relationship to God. You can infer who he is from what he does.

The works in question are not merely the miracles, though they include those. They are the totality of his ministry: healing the sick, forgiving sins, raising the dead, teaching with unprecedented authority, welcoming the outcast, confronting the powerful, demonstrating a perfect integration of wisdom and compassion that no merely human character could sustain consistently across a whole life under pressure. C.S. Lewis called this the logic of the options: liar, lunatic, or Lord. Someone who says what Jesus says and does what Jesus does is either consciously deceiving (liar), genuinely self-deceived (lunatic), or the truth (Lord). The middle position — admiring teacher, inspirational sage, enlightened moralist — is not actually available once you look at the full profile of the claims.

The disciples' question from the boat carries a different texture — it is not intellectual analysis but shaken awe. They are not reasoning; they are undone. The question is the response of people who have had their existing categories shattered and have not yet found new ones adequate to what they have witnessed. Who then is this? The "then" is crucial — it connects the question to the specific event that prompted it. Something just happened that makes the ordinary category of "teacher" or "prophet" or "healer" simply insufficient. Something just happened that required a different kind of answer than those categories can provide.

And that is precisely the experience this devotional is designed to cultivate and extend. Not the accumulation of a convincing argument for Christ's identity — though arguments have their place — but the steady accumulation of encounters with who he actually is, until the categories we brought to the project turn out to be too small and have to be replaced with larger ones. The disciples had been following him for months. They thought they knew who he was. And then the storm stopped at his word, and they looked at each other and realized they were still at the beginning.

We are at Day 48. We are still at the beginning. The question does not become less urgent with familiarity; it becomes more urgent, more specific, more weighted with what we have seen. Who then is this? The answer the New Testament gives — Son of God, Lord, Savior, Word made flesh, the Alpha and the Omega — is true. But it must not remain abstract. It must become, for each observer, the conclusion of their own sustained looking. The question is good. Keep asking it. The answer will keep growing larger.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus invites the skeptical to start with his works and reason toward his identity: "believe the works, that you may know." Is there something about the cumulative evidence of Christ's life and ministry that you have not yet fully reckoned with? What would honest reasoning from the evidence require you to conclude?
  2. The disciples asked "Who then is this?" as an expression of shaken awe rather than detached analysis. Has there been a moment in your engagement with Jesus — in these forty-eight days or before — when your existing categories were shattered and you found yourself asking the question from the inside of the experience rather than the outside? What was it?
  3. If the question "Who is this?" must never become merely an abstract doctrinal affirmation but always a living, growing, personally weighted encounter — what does that mean for the way you continue the remaining days of this devotional? What are you still hoping to see?

Going Deeper

The question of Jesus' identity has produced more sustained intellectual engagement than any other question in the history of human thought. The classical Christological debates of the first four centuries — Nicea (325), Constantinople (381), Ephesus (431), Chalcedon (451) — were not academic exercises; they were attempts to give precise and defensible answers to the question the disciples asked in the boat, using the philosophical tools available in the Greco-Roman world. The result — the Chalcedonian definition of one Person in two natures, fully divine and fully human — was not the imposition of Greek philosophy onto Jewish Christianity, but the careful preservation of the New Testament's own witness against inadequate alternatives.

The inadequate alternatives have always had a recognizable shape: either the divinity is real and the humanity is diminished (the Docetic and Monophysite errors), or the humanity is real and the divinity is reduced (the Arian and adoptionist errors). Every deviation from Chalcedon ultimately leaves you with a Jesus who cannot save — either too divine to have genuinely entered our condition, or too human to accomplish anything with eternal weight. The question "Who then is this?" has a precise answer that has been carefully worked out by the church over centuries: the one who spoke to the storm, who forgave sins, who rose from the dead, who the disciples fell before in fear — he is the eternal Son of God, the second Person of the Trinity, fully God and fully human, one undivided Person, who is the same yesterday and today and forever (Hebrews 13:8). The awe the disciples felt in the boat was not misplaced. It was the right response to the right answer to the question they had not yet had words for.
