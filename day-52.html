Day Fifty-Two

Lazarus, Come Out

John 11:43–44  ·  New Living Translation
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you stood before a sealed tomb and commanded a dead man to come out — and he came. Death had no answer for your word. It never has. We come today to this moment in your story knowing that the authority you demonstrated at Lazarus' tomb is the same authority by which you will raise every one of your people on the last day. Let the certainty of that future shape the way we live in the present. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Then Jesus shouted, 'Lazarus, come out!' And the dead man came out, his hands and feet bound in graveclothes, his face wrapped in a headcloth. Jesus told them, 'Unwrap him and let him go!'"
John 11:43–44, NLT

Four days. John specifies the detail deliberately: Lazarus has been in the tomb for four days. In first-century Jewish belief, the soul of the dead hovered near the body for three days — and after the third day, death was considered fully and irreversibly established. Four days is past ambiguity, past the possible misdiagnosis of deep coma, past any loophole through which a resuscitation might be rationalized as something other than what it is. John wants no doubt about the condition of the man in the tomb when the command comes.

Martha's warning — "Lord, by this time there will be an odor, for he has been dead four days" (John 11:39) — is the voice of practical realism confronting what she cannot yet believe is possible. She has already confessed, moments before, that Jesus is "the Christ, the Son of God, who is coming into the world" (11:27). She believes in the resurrection at the last day (11:24). What she cannot yet conceive is that the resurrection she believes in for the future is, right now, standing in front of her tomb and asking for the stone to be rolled away.

Jesus prays aloud — not for his own benefit but for the crowd's, explicitly: "I said this for the benefit of the people standing here, so that they will believe you sent me" (John 11:42). The prayer is a statement of relationship and transparency: he acts not independently but in full communion with the Father, not to secure power he lacks but to display the source of the power he has always had. And then, without ritual, without extended preparation, with the confidence of one who has never met a door that did not open at his word: Lazarus, come out.

The precision of the name matters. He does not shout a general command into the tomb. He calls one man by name — which is, as D.L. Moody once observed, a mercy: if he had said simply "Come out," every grave in the district would have emptied. He calls Lazarus specifically, personally, by name. This is how he calls his own: each one individually, known by name (John 10:3), each resurrection a personal encounter with the one who has the authority to give life to whom he will.

And then the extraordinary image: a dead man in grave clothes shuffling out of a dark cave. Still wrapped. Needing others to unwrap him. Alive but not yet free in all the practical ways that life requires. Jesus' final instruction — Unwrap him and let him go — involves the community. The miracle is his alone. The unwrapping requires others. There is a theology of the church embedded in this detail: Christ gives the life; the community of faith participates in the liberation of those who have been given it, the slow patient work of freeing one another from the grave clothes of old habits, old lies, old identities that the death of the old self no longer requires.

John tells us that this miracle was the immediate trigger for the Sanhedrin's decision to kill Jesus (John 11:47–53). The man who raised the dead was sentenced to die for it. The life-giver was put to death by the ones who could not bear what giving life exposed about their own condition. And then, of course, the tomb that was meant to hold him did precisely what Lazarus' tomb had done: it failed. You cannot contain the one who is himself the resurrection and the life. The command that called Lazarus out echoes, three days later, in the empty garden — called not by an angel into the darkness but by the life itself, rising from within.

For Reflection
  1. Martha believed in the resurrection at the last day but could not yet grasp that the resurrection was standing in front of her right now. Are there ways you hold true beliefs about Christ abstractly — as future realities or doctrinal propositions — without yet receiving them as present, personal, immediate? What would it mean to close the gap?
  2. "Lazarus, come out" — he calls by name. John 10:3 says the good shepherd calls his sheep by name. What does it mean to you that your relationship with Christ is personal and specific — that you are not an undifferentiated member of a category but known individually by the one who calls?
  3. Lazarus came out still wrapped in grave clothes; the community unwrapped him. In what ways are you still wearing grave clothes — the habits, fears, identities, or patterns that belong to the old life rather than the new? And who are the people in your life whom Christ has given the privilege of helping to unwrap you?

Going Deeper

The raising of Lazarus is the seventh and climactic sign in John's Gospel — the culmination of a series of miracles that have progressively disclosed who Jesus is. Water to wine (2:1–11) revealed his glory; the healing of the official's son at a distance (4:46–54) revealed his sovereign word; the healing at Bethesda (5:1–15) revealed his life-giving authority on the Sabbath; the feeding of the five thousand (6:1–14) revealed him as the bread of life; the walking on water (6:16–21) revealed his divine sovereignty over creation; the healing of the man born blind (9:1–41) revealed him as the light of the world. And now, the raising of Lazarus reveals him as the resurrection and the life — the one who has authority not merely over disease and disability but over death itself.

The theological significance of this final sign is that it establishes the ground of the Christian hope. Christ's authority over death is not asserted by argument or inferred from principle — it is demonstrated at a specific tomb, on a specific afternoon, before a large crowd of specific witnesses. The Reformed tradition's insistence on the bodily resurrection — of Lazarus, and supremely of Christ himself — rests on this evidential foundation. Paul argues explicitly in 1 Corinthians 15 that if there is no resurrection of the dead, then Christ has not been raised, and if Christ has not been raised, the faith is futile and we are still in our sins (15:17). The raising of Lazarus is the sign that points toward the only event in human history that makes every other Christian claim intelligible: a body coming out of a tomb, alive, on the third day. Death has met the one it cannot hold.
