Day Fifty-Five

Your Sins Are Forgiven

Mark 2:5, 9–11  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, four men tore a hole in a roof to lower their friend to you. They were willing to make a mess of someone else's house, to be conspicuous and disruptive, to do whatever it took to get their friend in front of you. When you looked at the paralyzed man, you saw what his friends and the crowd could not see: the deeper damage beneath the visible one. And you addressed that first. Give us eyes to see what you see — in ourselves, and in those we love. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And when Jesus saw their faith, he said to the paralytic, 'Son, your sins are forgiven.'… 'Which is easier, to say to the paralytic, "Your sins are forgiven," or to say, "Rise, take up your bed and walk"? But that you may know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins' — he said to the paralytic — 'I say to you, rise, pick up your bed, and go home.'"
Mark 2:5, 9–11, ESV

A paralyzed man is lowered through a roof into the middle of a crowd, in front of Jesus. The friends' ingenuity — tiles removed, ropes lowered — is an act of faith so obvious that Mark notes Jesus saw it and responded to it. And then the most unexpected thing happens: Jesus looks at the man who has been carried here for physical healing and says, Son, your sins are forgiven.

The man asked for nothing. The friends presumably wanted a healing. The crowd wanted to see what would happen. And Jesus, who knew what was in people (John 2:25), looked at this man and addressed the deeper wound first. Whether this was specific to this man's situation — whether his paralysis was connected to guilt in some way that the text does not explain — or whether it represents Jesus' consistent priority of the spiritual over the physical, the result is the same: the most important thing about this man's condition, in Jesus' assessment, is not the legs that don't work but the sin that has separated him from God.

The scribes sitting there understand the claim exactly: Who can forgive sins but God alone? (Mark 2:7). They are right about the logic. Forgiveness of sin is not like the forgiveness of a personal offense between two parties, where the one wronged has the standing to forgive. Sin, at its root, is an offense against God — against the one whose law has been violated, whose glory has been diminished, whose image has been marred. Only God can forgive it, because only God occupies the position of the party most fundamentally wronged. When Jesus says "your sins are forgiven," he is either committing the most egregious blasphemy possible, or he is the one to whom the authority genuinely belongs.

He answers their unspoken challenge with a question: which is easier to say? The answer is obvious: it is easier to say "your sins are forgiven" because no one can verify it. Walking is visible. Forgiveness is invisible. But he offers the visible as evidence of the invisible: that you may know that the Son of Man has authority on earth to forgive sins — and then he heals the man. The physical healing is not the point; it is the proof. The point is the forgiveness. He has the authority to address what no doctor, no priest under the old covenant, no one except God could address: the rupture between a human being and their Creator.

This is what all the other healings, miracles, and acts of authority have been building toward. He stills storms because he made them. He heals bodies because he made them. He raises the dead because he is the resurrection and the life. And he forgives sins because he is God — and because, within weeks of this encounter, he will take the sins he is forgiving onto himself at the cross, bearing in his own body the cost of every word of forgiveness he ever spoke. The authority to forgive is not merely the authority to declare; it is the authority to absorb. He says "your sins are forgiven" at Capernaum with the full knowledge of what it will cost him to make that word true.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus addressed the man's spiritual condition before the physical one — "your sins are forgiven" before "rise and walk." What does this order of priority reveal about how Jesus sees human beings? And is there a deeper wound in your own life that you have been asking Christ to address after the surface problems rather than first?
  2. The scribes objected: "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" They were right about the logic. The only possible responses to Jesus forgiving sins are that he was a blasphemer or that he was God. How does this binary land for you — and what have you done with it?
  3. The physical healing was given as visible evidence of the invisible forgiveness. The forgiveness was the real miracle; the healing was the proof. In your own experience of Christ's grace, what has he given you that is visible — that you can point to as evidence of the deeper invisible work happening in you?

Going Deeper

The scribes' objection in Mark 2:7 — "Who can forgive sins but God alone?" — is one of the clearest examples in the Synoptic Gospels of an implicit divine claim being embedded in Jesus' action rather than explicitly stated in his words. He does not say "I am God and therefore I forgive you." He says "your sins are forgiven" — in the divine passive, which the scribes read correctly as a claim to divine authority. The claim is no less bold for being indirect; if anything, the indirection is more theologically precise, because it identifies forgiveness as something that stands in God's realm and that no human mediator can simply pronounce on their own authority.

This connects directly to the doctrine of propitiation — the satisfaction of God's justice in the atonement. Christ can forgive sins on his own authority because he is the one who will bear their penalty. The forgiveness he pronounces at Capernaum is not declared without cost; it is declared in advance of the payment, by the one who knows he will make it. Paul writes in Romans 3:25–26 that God presented Christ as a propitiation "to demonstrate his righteousness… so that he would be just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus." The forgiveness is just because the penalty has been borne — not evaded, not overlooked, not reduced, but fully absorbed by the one who pronounced the forgiveness. Every word of "your sins are forgiven" that Jesus ever spoke was a promissory note on what Golgotha would accomplish. The cross is the moment when all those notes were honored at once.
