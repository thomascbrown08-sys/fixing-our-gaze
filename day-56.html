Day Fifty-Six

The Good Shepherd

John 10:11, 14–15  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, you called yourself the good shepherd and defined the goodness not by skill or success but by sacrifice: "the good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep." The hireling runs when danger comes. You ran toward it. Let this definition of goodness — love that does not protect itself at the expense of those it loves — shape what we understand by love in every other context. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"I am the good shepherd. The good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep… I am the good shepherd. I know my own and my own know me, just as the Father knows me and I know the Father; and I lay down my life for the sheep."
John 10:11, 14–15, ESV

The good shepherd passage unfolds in layers. There is the sheep and the shepherd, the gate and the fold, the voice that the sheep know and the stranger's voice they flee. But the definition Jesus offers for what makes a shepherd good strips everything else away and comes to a single, decisive point: the good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep. Every other quality of the good shepherd — the knowledge, the voice, the care, the searching — flows from and toward this central act. He is good because he gives himself.

The contrast with the hireling clarifies everything. The hireling is not malicious; he does his job when the job is easy. But when the wolf comes, when the cost becomes personal, the hireling discovers that the sheep are not his — they are someone else's, and someone else's risk is not his problem. He flees. The sheep are scattered. The wolf is satisfied. This is the shape of a love that has conditions, that persists up to the point of personal cost and then terminates. It is not evil; it is merely human at its most honest about its own limits.

Jesus says: I am not the hireling. I will not flee. The wolf that comes for my sheep will find me between them and itself, and I will lay down my life before I let it have them. The cross is not a theological abstraction here — it is the shepherd's body between the wolf and the flock. Every one of his sheep was bought at the price of his own blood. The goodness is not theoretical; it has a specific, historical, irreversible event behind it that the sheep can point to and say: there is the evidence. There is what it cost him to keep us.

Then comes the depth charge: just as the Father knows me and I know the Father. He is drawing a direct parallel between the Father-Son relationship and the shepherd-sheep relationship — placing the intimacy of the eternal Trinity as the template for the intimacy he offers his people. The knowledge between Father and Son is not informational familiarity but the full interpersonal knowing of persons in eternal, unbroken relationship. And this is the quality of knowing that he is extending to his sheep: I know my own. Not cataloguing them from a distance. Knowing them with the kind of knowing that exists between the Father and the Son.

John 10:3 adds the detail we noted at Lazarus' tomb: "He calls his own sheep by name." Each sheep is individually known. The flock is not an undifferentiated mass cared for in bulk. Every one is named, every one is specifically led out, every one hears a call that is addressed personally. The authority of the shepherd is not impersonal cosmic governance. It is intimate individual care, extended to every sheep by name, sustained by the life that was laid down to keep each one.

And the shepherd is also the gate (John 10:9): I am the door of the sheep. If anyone enters by me, he will be saved and will go in and out and find pasture. The safety and the access and the provision all run through the same Person. There is no back door, no alternative fold, no other path to the pasture that bypasses the one who laid down his life at the entrance. He is the way in, the guardian of the space, and the one who provides what is found inside. The whole ecosystem of belonging runs through him.

For Reflection
  1. The good shepherd is defined by what he does when the wolf comes — when loyalty becomes costly. In your own relationships and commitments, where do you function more like the hireling than the shepherd — present when the cost is low, absent when it rises? What would shepherd-love look like in those situations?
  2. "I know my own and my own know me" — the knowing is mutual, modeled on the Father-Son relationship. How well do you know the shepherd? Not about him — him. What does it mean to know Christ in the personal, intimate sense he is describing here, rather than simply knowing correct things about him?
  3. He calls his sheep by name — each one individually, not in bulk. How does it land for you to know that you are not a member of a category being generally managed, but a specific person called by name by the one who laid down his life? Does this feel real to you — and if not, what prevents it?

Going Deeper

John 10:11 — "the good shepherd lays down his life for the sheep" — introduces one of the most important prepositions in the theology of the atonement: hyper, "for," "on behalf of," "in place of." Throughout John's Gospel this word carries a substitutionary weight: "the bread which I shall give for the life of the world" (6:51), Caiaphas' unwitting prophecy that "it is better for you that one man should die for the people" (11:50), Jesus' own words at the Last Supper, "my body given for you" (Luke 22:19). The shepherd does not die alongside the sheep or because of the sheep — he dies in their place, between them and the wolf, taking the blow that was heading for them.

John 10:17–18 adds the dimension of voluntary self-giving that distinguishes this death from all others: "I lay down my life that I may take it up again. No one takes it from me, but I lay it down of my own accord. I have authority to lay it down, and I have authority to take it up again." The cross is not a tragedy that overtook Jesus despite his best efforts; it is an act of sovereign freedom. He goes to the cross as the good shepherd goes between the wolf and the flock — not compelled from without but driven from within, by the love that will not abandon its own. The authority that healed the sick and raised the dead and stilled the storm is the same authority that lays down its life: not overcome by death, but freely entering it, on behalf of the sheep, with the full intention of walking back out the other side.
