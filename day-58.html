Day Fifty-Eight

I and the Father Are One

John 10:30  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, Son, and Spirit — one God, three Persons, the eternal community of love from which all creation springs and into which, by grace, we are drawn. We approach this mystery today not to fully comprehend it — we cannot — but to kneel before it, to receive what it means for our security and our worship, and to let it do in us what only the reality of the Trinity can do: make us one as they are one. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"I and the Father are one."
John 10:30, ESV

Five words in English, four in Greek — egō kai ho Patēr hen esmen — and another moment when stones are lifted to throw. The crowd's response is the surest interpretive guide to what they heard: they took it as blasphemy. Not metaphorical unity of purpose — that claim would not have moved anyone toward the stone pile. They understood Jesus to be claiming identity with God, claiming to be in such a relation to the Father that the two together constitute a single divine reality. And they were right about what he claimed. They were wrong only about whether the claim was true.

The statement comes in the context of the good shepherd discourse, and specifically in response to the crowd's demand for a plain declaration: "How long will you keep us in suspense? If you are the Christ, tell us plainly" (John 10:24). What they want is a clear, declarative sentence. He gives them one — though not the form they expected. Instead of "I am the Christ," he says: My sheep hear my voice, and I know them, and they follow me. I give them eternal life, and they will never perish, and no one will snatch them out of my hand. My Father, who has given them to me, is greater than all, and no one is able to snatch them out of the Father's hand. I and the Father are one (10:27–30).

The declaration arrives as the theological capstone of a security statement. He is saying: your security rests in my hand and in the Father's hand simultaneously — because my hand and the Father's hand are the same hand. The sheep are held by the Son, and the Son and the Father are one, so the sheep are held by the Father, and no one is greater than the Father, so the hold is absolute. The unity of the Father and the Son is what makes the promise of "they will never perish" credible. If the Son and the Father were two separate beings of different power and not essentially one, there would always be something greater than one of them that could threaten the sheep. But there is not. The one who holds is also the one who is all-sufficient. Nothing escapes the double hold of what is, at its deepest level, one.

The Greek neuter in hen — "one" — is theologically precise and has been carefully noticed. Jesus does not say heis, which would mean numerically one person. He says hen — one in essence, one in nature, one in being. The Father and the Son are not the same person — Jesus speaks to the Father, prays to the Father, is sent by the Father. They are distinct persons. But they are one in what they are: one God, one nature, one divine essence, one will, one power, one love.

This is not a comfortable or easy claim. It was not received as comfortable or easy by those who heard it. It is either true or it is the most audacious lie ever told. There is no version of this claim that can be received as spiritually impressive but not quite divine, as elevated but not excessive, as close enough to God to be inspiring without being the actual thing. I and the Father are one. He means it in the strongest possible sense. The crowd at his feet knew that, and lifted stones. The church has knelt before it for two thousand years instead.

For Reflection
  1. The declaration "I and the Father are one" was made in the context of a security promise: nothing can snatch you from my hand or the Father's hand, because they are the same hand. What does it mean for your sense of security in Christ to rest on this? Not on his goodwill alone, but on the omnipotence of the one who is Father and Son together?
  2. The crowd demanded plainness and received it — but not in the form they expected. They wanted "I am the Christ" and got "I and the Father are one," which was more than they bargained for. Are there ways you have wanted a manageable Christ — a Christ who is great and inspiring without being fully divine? What does this claim do to that preference?
  3. "No one will snatch them out of my hand." Is there a fear about your own standing before God — a worry that you might somehow be lost, that grace might ultimately prove insufficient for your particular case — that this double-hand security speaks directly to? What would it mean to rest your security specifically in this promise?

Going Deeper

John 10:30 is one of the most critical texts in the development of Trinitarian doctrine. The careful parsing of hen (neuter — one in essence) versus heis (masculine — one person) was used by the Nicene theologians to establish the consubstantiality of the Father and the Son: they are one in substance (homoousios), distinct in person (hypostasis). Athanasius pointed to this verse repeatedly in his defense of Nicene orthodoxy against Arius, who wanted to read the unity of Father and Son as a unity of will or purpose — making the Son a supremely obedient creature rather than a co-equal Person of the divine being.

The practical stakes of this debate are enormous, as we have noted before (Days 3 and 12). If the Son is only united with the Father in will and purpose but not in nature, then John 10:28–30's security promise is weaker than it appears — because "my hand and the Father's hand" would be two separate hands belonging to beings of different power, and the security of the sheep would ultimately rest on the weaker of the two. But if the Son and the Father are one in being — if holding a sheep in the Son's hand is identical to holding it in the Father's hand, because the Son's hand and the Father's hand are the same divine omnipotence — then the promise is absolute. Your security is not in the greatest power you know of, next to which there might be something greater. It is in the one power that is simply the greatest, period, unlimited, unconditioned, holding you with both hands that are one hand. No one can snatch you from that.
