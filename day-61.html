Day Sixty-One

The Anointing at Bethany

Mark 14:3–9  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, we begin the final approach. The cross is no longer on the horizon — it is days away. This woman saw it coming before the disciples did, and she did the only thing that seemed adequate: she broke the jar. She held nothing back. Give us something of her understanding, her urgency, her willingness to be thought wasteful for loving you without calculation. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And while he was at Bethany in the house of Simon the leper, as he was reclining at table, a woman came with an alabaster flask of ointment of pure nard, very costly, and she broke it and poured it over his head. There were some who said to themselves indignantly, 'Why was the ointment wasted like this? For this ointment could have been sold for more than three hundred denarii and given to the poor.'… But Jesus said, 'Leave her alone. Why do you trouble her? She has done a beautiful thing to me.'"
Mark 14:3–6, ESV

Six days before Passover. Jerusalem is crowded with pilgrims and restless with expectation. The chief priests have already met and decided that Jesus must die (Mark 14:1–2). The disciple who will betray him is already in negotiation (14:10–11). The cross is a week away. And in this compressed, darkening moment, a woman comes into a house in Bethany with an alabaster jar worth a year's wages — and breaks it.

She does not pour a measured amount. She does not apply the ointment with clinical precision. She breaks the flask, which means there is no stopping, no reclaiming what has been given, no possibility of using only half and reserving the rest. Everything or nothing. She chose everything. The ointment runs down his head and fills the room with the kind of fragrance that will not leave quickly — the fragrance of an extravagance that has gone past calculation into worship.

The indignation is immediate. Some of those present are angry — not at the excess of love but at the apparent waste of resources. Three hundred denarii. Nearly a year's wages for an ordinary laborer. Imagine the poor who could have eaten for months. Imagine what could have been done. They are not entirely wrong in their arithmetic. The poor are real, the need is real, and three hundred denarii is three hundred denarii. What they cannot calculate is the singular, unrepeatable nature of the moment. Jesus reads it: she has done a beautiful thing to me.

The word translated "beautiful" — kalos — carries both moral and aesthetic weight in Greek. It means good, but also lovely, fitting, done rightly, in a manner appropriate to the moment. What she has done is not merely generous; it is right in the way that only someone who understood the moment could have made it right. She is, Jesus says, anointing his body for burial (14:8). She knows. In a room of followers who have not yet grasped what is coming, this woman has seen it — and her response to seeing it is to pour out the costliest thing she has, without remainder, over the one who is about to pour out his life without remainder for her.

Jesus says something that has been true for two thousand years and shows no sign of becoming less true: wherever the gospel is proclaimed in the whole world, what she has done will be told in memory of her. She has no name in Mark. She is not famous. She occupies two verses in a chapter largely concerned with conspiracy and betrayal. And yet her act, because it was seen and received and honored by the one she poured it over, has been retold in every language on earth, in every generation, in every place where the gospel has reached. Extravagant love directed at the right object is never wasted — not by the measure of the one who receives it.

We enter the passion now. Let this woman's gesture be the posture of the days ahead: nothing held back, the jar broken, the ointment poured without calculation. These ten days require a different kind of attention than the sections that came before them. What is about to happen to Jesus cannot be understood quickly or efficiently. It must be sat with. Let us break our own jars and pour.

For Reflection
  1. Those who objected to the woman's act were not wrong about the poor or about the cost. They were wrong about the moment and the Person. Are there ways you have brought calculations of efficiency or practicality to your love for Christ — measuring what you give rather than breaking the jar? What would uncalculated love look like for you?
  2. She broke the flask — making the gift irreversible, total, without the possibility of reclaiming what was offered. What is the "flask" in your life that you have not yet broken — the thing you have kept sealed, available for a less costly moment? What is preventing you from breaking it?
  3. Jesus said her act would be told wherever the gospel is proclaimed. The thing the room thought wasteful, he declared permanently memorial. What does this say about the difference between the world's economy of value and the kingdom's? Can you think of an act of love that seemed wasteful by ordinary measures but turned out to be exactly right?

Going Deeper

The four Gospel accounts of the anointing present variations that have led scholars to ask whether they describe one event or two. Mark and Matthew describe an unnamed woman anointing Jesus' head at a dinner in Bethany near the end of his ministry. John names the woman as Mary of Bethany (Lazarus's sister) and describes the anointing of his feet. Luke describes a similar anointing earlier in the ministry by "a woman who was a sinner" in a Pharisee's house. The dominant view in the Reformed tradition is that there are two anointings: an earlier one (Luke) and a later one (Mark, Matthew, John describing the same event from different angles, with John providing the name). The differences in detail are consistent with independent eyewitness accounts of the same event.

What all accounts share is the theological center: extravagant, costly, uninhibited devotion to Christ, received by him as appropriate and honored by him as permanently significant. The Reformers saw in this passage a rebuke to any theology that subordinates direct love for Christ to utilitarian calculation — the heresy of reducing the entire Christian life to social usefulness and forgetting that the first and greatest commandment is love for God, from which love for neighbor flows but which it does not replace. The woman's act is the enacted form of the first commandment: she loved the Lord her God with all her heart, soul, mind, and strength — and the strength here was measured in three hundred denarii of pure nard. In the economy of the kingdom, nothing poured over him is ever lost.
