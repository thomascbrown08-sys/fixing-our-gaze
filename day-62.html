Day Sixty-Two

The Upper Room

John 13:4–5, 12–15  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, on the night of your arrest you wrapped yourself in a towel and knelt at your disciples' feet. Knowing everything that was coming. Knowing that the one whose feet you were washing would betray you within hours. You washed his feet too. Let the full weight of this settle on us before we move on. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Jesus… rose from supper. He laid aside his outer garments, and taking a towel, tied it around his waist. Then he poured water into a basin and began to wash the disciples' feet and to wipe them with the towel that was wrapped around him… 'Do you understand what I have done to you? You call me Teacher and Lord, and you are right, for so I am. If I then, your Lord and Teacher, have washed your feet, you also ought to wash one another's feet. For I have given you an example, that you also should do just as I have done to you.'"
John 13:4–5, 12–15, ESV

John begins his account of the Last Supper with a sentence that holds everything: "Having loved his own who were in the world, he loved them to the end" (13:1). The Greek word eis telos — to the end, to the uttermost — gathers into it both the temporal meaning (he loved them until the end, all the way to the cross) and the qualitative meaning (he loved them completely, to the fullest possible extent). The footwashing that follows is the first demonstration of what loving to the uttermost looks like in practice. And the cross will be the last.

He rises from the table. He lays aside his outer garments. He ties a towel around his waist. He pours water into a basin. Each action is deliberate, unhurried, specific. John is not summarizing; he is watching, and he wants us to watch with him. The Teacher and Lord kneels on the floor of an upper room and begins to wash feet — feet dirty from the Jerusalem streets, feet belonging to people who have spent the evening arguing about which of them is greatest (Luke 22:24 tells us this argument happened at this same supper).

He washes them all. Including Judas, who has already been to the chief priests (Mark 14:10–11) and whose betrayal Jesus knows is imminent (John 13:21). The towel moves from person to person without the love being rationed or withheld on the basis of who deserves it. The one who will deny him gets clean feet. The one who will betray him gets clean feet. This is not naivety — he knows exactly what they are going to do. It is love whose character is not conditioned by the behavior of the beloved.

When Peter objects — "You shall never wash my feet" — Jesus answers that unless Peter allows this, he has no part with him. Peter swings immediately to the opposite extreme: then wash all of me. And Jesus responds with patient precision: "The one who has bathed does not need to wash, except for his feet, but is completely clean" (13:10). The disciples have been bathed — the deeper cleansing of forgiveness and regeneration that is the work of the Spirit. What they need is the ongoing cleansing of the feet — the repeated, humble receiving of grace for the daily dirt that accumulates on the journey. The basin is always available. The towel is never put away.

"I have given you an example, that you also should do just as I have done to you." The footwashing is not a liturgical institution (though it has been observed as one in some traditions). It is a pattern: the greatest serving the least, the Teacher stooping below the student, the Lord taking the position of the household slave. In the kingdom of the one who washed feet on the night he was arrested, greatness runs downward. The basin and the towel are its instruments.

He eats with them then — the Passover meal reinterpreted. He takes the bread and breaks it: this is my body. He takes the cup: this cup is the new covenant in my blood. The meal that had commemorated Israel's liberation from Egypt is now redefined around his body and blood — the new exodus, the final liberation, accomplished not through the death of an animal but through the death of the Lamb who sat at table with his friends and washed their feet and loved them to the end.

For Reflection
  1. He washed Judas's feet, knowing what Judas was about to do. Love that is conditioned on the beloved's behavior is not the love he demonstrated in that upper room. Where in your own relationships do you find it hardest to extend love that is not conditioned on the other person's response? What does his example press you toward?
  2. "The one who has bathed needs only to wash his feet" — the deeper cleansing is done; the daily accumulation remains. How do you experience the ongoing need for the basin and towel in your own life? What does humble, repeated return to grace look like for you practically?
  3. He rose from supper to wash feet on the night of his arrest. With everything pressing in on him — the betrayal hours away, the cross days away — he spent this time stooping. What does this tell you about what he considers most important when time is short and the stakes are highest?

Going Deeper

The footwashing in John 13 has a double significance that the text itself makes explicit. At one level, it is a moral example: "I have given you an example, that you also should do just as I have done to you" (13:15). Humility, servant-leadership, and mutual care in the community are concretely modeled and commanded. At another level, Jesus' statement to Peter — "If I do not wash you, you have no share with me" (13:8) — indicates that the washing carries a deeper symbolic weight: participation in Christ, cleansing from sin, the grace that makes fellowship with him possible at all.

The Reformed tradition has generally resisted reading too sharp a distinction between these two levels — not because the symbolic is unimportant but because the moral follows from and is empowered by the theological. You wash one another's feet because he has washed yours. The grace that cleanses enables the service that models. This is the consistent pattern of the New Testament's ethics: the indicative (what Christ has done) grounds and produces the imperative (what his people do). "Be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving one another, as God in Christ forgave you" (Ephesians 4:32). The as is always doing enormous work — you do not generate the forgiveness from within yourself; you extend what you have received. The basin and towel are borrowed from the one who first used them on you.
