Day Sixty-Seven

It Is Finished

John 19:28–30  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, with one word you declared the work complete. Not abandoned, not interrupted, not merely ended — finished. The Greek word means accomplished, fulfilled, brought to its appointed conclusion. Six thousand years of human sin, four thousand years of sacrificial anticipation, every promise and every type and every shadow — all of it gathered into this moment and this word. Teach us to receive it as finished rather than treating it as something that still needs our contribution. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"After this, Jesus, knowing that all was now finished, said (to fulfill the Scripture), 'I thirst.' A jar full of sour wine stood there, so they put a sponge full of the sour wine on a hyssop branch and held it to his mouth. When Jesus had received the sour wine, he said, 'It is finished,' and he bowed his head and gave up his spirit."
John 19:28–30, ESV

Three hours of darkness. The soldiers casting lots for his clothing. His mother and the beloved disciple at the foot of the cross. The taunt of the passing crowd: "If you are the Son of God, come down" — the same temptation offered in the desert, now offered again from below the cross rather than above the pinnacle. He did not come down in the wilderness. He does not come down here. He stays, because staying is the whole point.

John tells us: knowing that all was now finished. He tracks the approach to the end with a kind of conscious purposefulness — one more Scripture to fulfill (Psalm 69:21, the sour wine), and then the word. He does not slip into death. He does not lose consciousness and cease. He speaks, and bows his head, and gives up his spirit. The verb — paredōken — means he handed it over. Voluntarily. The life that no one could take (John 10:18) is given, at the moment and in the manner of his own choosing, with a final word of completion on his lips.

Tetelestai. One word in Greek. Perfect tense — an action completed with ongoing effect. Not "it was finished" (as if it were over and past) but "it is finished and stands finished" — a permanent state of completion that continues from this moment forward. The word was used in commerce for a debt fully paid: written across an invoice or a record of obligation, it meant the account was settled, the claim was satisfied, nothing more was owed. It was used in construction for a building completed. In religious contexts, for a sacrifice brought to its proper conclusion.

Everything this word encompasses is almost too large to hold in a single meditation. The sacrificial system of Israel — centuries of morning and evening offerings, the Day of Atonement, the Passover, the bulls and goats whose blood could never finally remove sin (Hebrews 10:4) — finished. The promises to Abraham, to David, to the prophets who saw from a distance the suffering servant who would bear the iniquities of many (Isaiah 53:11) — finished. The Father's eternal plan, resolved in the pactum salutis before creation, to send the Son into the far country of human lostness and bring his people home — finished. The active obedience of a sinless life lived in our place, every demand of the law met, every requirement of perfect righteousness fulfilled — finished. The passive obedience of the cross, the bearing of the cup of wrath, the hours of God-forsakenness that paid the penalty our sin incurred — finished.

Finished is the exact opposite of what the disciples heard on Good Friday. To them, the word surely felt like failure — another prophet executed, another movement crushed, another hope gone to the tomb. What they did not yet understand was that the finish line had been reached, not the cliffside fallen from. The race was not cut short. The race was completed. And the one who said "it is finished" said it as a victor, not as a victim — with enough breath left to speak clearly, with his mind intact, with the full weight of what he had accomplished in his understanding.

You are not contributing to this. You cannot. The work is finished. What you receive is not a partial payment that your effort supplements. It is the finished thing — the accomplished redemption, the settled account, the completed sacrifice — offered to you, freely, as a gift from the one who bowed his head and gave up his spirit when the work was done.

For Reflection
  1. Tetelestai — "it stands finished," permanently, with ongoing effect. Do you live as though the work is finished, or as though it still requires your completion — your sufficient obedience, your adequate repentance, your appropriate spiritual performance? What would change if the finished nature of this work became the ground of your daily standing before God?
  2. He "gave up his spirit" — he handed it over voluntarily. Even his death was an act of sovereignty. What does it mean for your understanding of the cross that he chose the moment, spoke the completion, and then released his life? Does it change how you receive what he accomplished?
  3. The disciples heard "it is finished" as failure before they understood it as victory. Has there been something in your own story that appeared to be the end — an ending that felt like defeat — which later turned out to be a completion? How does that experience illuminate what happened at Golgotha?

Going Deeper

The word tetelestai in John 19:30 is the perfect passive indicative of teleō — "to complete, fulfill, bring to its appointed end." Its use in the commercial papyri of the first century — stamped across bills as a receipt marking full payment — has been well documented in archaeological discoveries, which gives the metaphor of debt settlement a concrete historical grounding. When Jesus says the work is finished, he is not using a vague or impressionistic expression. He is using the specific language of completed obligation: everything that was owed has been paid, everything that was required has been supplied, every account has been settled.

The Reformed tradition's insistence on the sufficiency and finality of Christ's atonement — that his sacrifice was complete, once for all, never to be repeated or supplemented (Hebrews 9:25–28; 10:10–14) — finds its clearest warrant in this single word. The Westminster Confession (8.5) states that Christ "made a proper, real, and full satisfaction to his Father's justice" — not a partial satisfaction, not a down-payment requiring human cooperation to complete, but a full, final, once-for-all settlement. This is why the Reformers rejected any understanding of the Mass as a re-presentation or continuation of the sacrifice: the sacrifice is finished. The priest has sat down (Hebrews 10:12) — the posture of one whose work is complete. The only appropriate human response to a finished work is to receive it, rest in it, and live from it rather than toward it.
