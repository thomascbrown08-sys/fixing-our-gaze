Day Seventy-One

He Appeared to Mary

John 20:14–16  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, Mary stood weeping outside the empty tomb, and you appeared to her — and she did not recognize you until you said her name. One word. Her name, spoken in your voice, and she knew. We begin the final section of this devotional with this moment because it is the shape of everything that follows: the risen Christ, speaking our names, calling us out of grief and confusion and into recognition. Say our names today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Turning around, she saw Jesus standing there, but she did not know that it was Jesus. Jesus said to her, 'Woman, why are you weeping? Whom are you seeking?' Supposing him to be the gardener, she said to him, 'Sir, if you have carried him away, tell me where you have laid him, and I will take him away.' Jesus said to her, 'Mary.' She turned and said to him in Aramaic, 'Rabboni!' (which means Teacher)."
John 20:14–16, ESV

We enter Section VI carrying everything we have seen. For ten days we watched the passion — the anointing, the upper room, the garden, the arrest, the trials, the cross, the tomb. Now the stone is open, the linen cloths are folded, and a woman is standing outside the tomb weeping. She has looked in and seen angels. She has been told he is risen. She has not understood it yet. She turns, and there is a man standing there whom she takes to be the gardener.

She does not recognize him. This is not a failure of eyesight or of devotion — she loved him more consistently than most of the disciples did; she is here before dawn while most of them are behind locked doors. The failure of recognition belongs to the nature of the resurrection body: it is genuinely his, continuous with the body that was buried, but transformed in a way that makes recognition require something beyond physical sight. He looked, evidently, like someone she had not seen before. The gardener.

He asks her two questions. Why are you weeping? Whom are you seeking? The questions carry a gentleness that is also a probe — the same probe that runs through the whole of John's Gospel. Every encounter with Jesus involves the question of who you are looking for and whether you have found him. The crowds in John 6 are seeking the wrong thing (bread that perishes). The soldiers in the garden sought him with torches and fell. And Mary is seeking a body — the Jesus she knew, the one she can anoint and tend and mourn properly. She is seeking the dead.

And then he says her name. One word. Mary. And the whole edifice of grief and confusion collapses into recognition: Rabboni. Teacher. The word she used when he was alive. The word that means she knows who she is talking to. Not an idea of him, not a spiritual impression, not a vision — him. The same one who called her by name before (Luke 8:2 suggests she was among those he healed), the same one she had followed, the same voice, the same knowledge of her name and how to say it in a way that reaches past everything else and lands.

John 10:3 — the good shepherd calls his sheep by name. We heard it sixty days ago. Here it is, embodied on a Sunday morning outside an empty tomb. He calls her name and she knows the voice. This is the first resurrection appearance, and it is given not to a council of elders or a gathering of priests or a crowd of five hundred but to a weeping woman who refused to leave the empty tomb. The first witness to the risen Christ is someone the ancient world would have dismissed as an unreliable witness by reason of gender alone. He chose her first. As he had chosen her before, and the leper before her, and the paralytic, and Zacchaeus in the tree — the pattern holds even in the resurrection morning. He goes to the ones who are there, who are broken open with love and grief and will not leave, and he says their name.

He says yours too. Not in the manner of a general announcement to all humanity, but specifically, personally, in the way that only a shepherd who knows his sheep can say a name. That is the risen Christ of the Gospels, and that is the Christ of the next twenty days.

For Reflection
  1. Mary did not recognize Jesus until he said her name. Recognition came through being known, not through her own perception. Is there a way you have been straining to perceive Christ through your own spiritual effort — and what would it look like to receive recognition as a gift, to wait for the voice that calls your name rather than achieving the encounter yourself?
  2. He appeared first to Mary — not to the apostles, not to the powerful, but to a woman weeping outside an empty tomb at dawn. What does this consistent pattern of Christ appearing to the grieving, the marginal, the ones who refused to leave tell you about who the resurrection is for and how it tends to arrive?
  3. She called him Rabboni — Teacher — the word she used before his death. The resurrection did not make him someone new; it revealed more fully who he always was. As you enter the final section of this devotional, what name do you find yourself using for him — and has that name grown or changed across the seventy days you have spent fixing your gaze?

Going Deeper

Mary Magdalene's role as the first witness to the resurrection has sometimes been described as the "apostle to the apostles" — a title used by early church fathers including Hippolytus of Rome and Thomas Aquinas, based on Jesus' commission to her: "Go to my brothers and say to them, 'I am ascending to my Father and your Father, to my God and your God'" (John 20:17). She is entrusted with the announcement of the resurrection before any of the Twelve. This choice is theologically deliberate rather than incidental — it is consistent with the pattern throughout the Gospels of Jesus honoring those whom his culture marginalized, and it establishes the resurrection as an event witnessed by those who were present out of love rather than by those who held institutional authority.

The scene also raises the question of continuity between the pre- and post-resurrection Christ — a question the appearances as a whole address from multiple angles. The risen Jesus is recognizably the same Person: he has the same voice, the same knowledge of people's names and histories, the same scars (John 20:27), the same capacity for physical presence (he eats with them, Luke 24:41–43). Yet he is also different: he appears in a locked room (John 20:19), is not always immediately recognized (Luke 24:16), and is described by Paul as a "spiritual body" (1 Corinthians 15:44) — not immaterial but transformed, the first fruits of the resurrection that all believers will share. He is not a ghost and not a resuscitated corpse. He is the firstborn from the dead (Colossians 1:18) — the new thing that the whole creation has been groaning toward, arrived now in the person of the gardener standing in the garden on the first morning of the new creation.
