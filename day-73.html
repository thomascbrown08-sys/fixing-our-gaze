Day Seventy-Three

Doubting Thomas

John 20:27–29  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, Thomas said he would not believe unless he could see and touch the wounds. A week later you appeared specifically for him, and you offered him exactly what he asked for. You did not rebuke him for asking. You came back for the one who struggled to believe with what the others had given him. Come back for those of us who struggle today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Then he said to Thomas, 'Put your finger here, and see my hands; and put out your hand, and place it in my side. Do not disbelieve, but believe.' Thomas answered him, 'My Lord and my God!' Jesus said to him, 'Have you believed because you have seen me? Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed.'"
John 20:27–29, ESV

Thomas was not present when the risen Christ appeared to the gathered disciples on Easter evening. When the others tell him — we have seen the Lord — he declines to believe on the strength of their testimony alone. He sets conditions: unless I see in his hands the mark of the nails, and place my finger into the mark of the nails, and place my hand into his side, I will never believe. The conditions are specific, empirical, tactile. He will not accept secondhand testimony for a firsthand claim. He is asking for exactly what the other disciples received: a direct encounter, not a reported one.

The risen Christ comes back a week later. The doors are locked again. He stands among them again. And this time he goes directly to Thomas, and he addresses Thomas's conditions one by one: Put your finger here. See my hands. Put out your hand and place it in my side. He knows what Thomas said, which means he was there when Thomas said it, unseen, present in the locked room when the doors were shut. He has heard the doubt and has come back specifically to address it.

There is enormous grace in this. Jesus does not exclude Thomas for his unwillingness to believe on secondhand testimony. He does not find another disciple to replace him. He returns, in person, exactly to the place of the doubt, and he offers the hands and the side. He carries the wounds of the cross in his resurrection body — not as damage that survived healing, but as marks that were kept deliberately, that are features of his identity now, that are part of who the risen Lord is. The scars are permanent because what they represent is permanent: he is always and forever the Lamb who was slain (Revelation 5:6).

Thomas's response is the highest confession in John's Gospel: My Lord and my God. He addresses Jesus directly with the divine name, using the possessive that makes it personal — not "a lord" or "the Lord God" but my. The one who was willing to say he would not believe on secondhand testimony now makes the most direct Christological confession in the New Testament. The doubt was deep; the faith that came from the encounter is correspondingly total.

And then Jesus says something for us: Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed. This beatitude — the last beatitude in the Gospels — is addressed not to anyone in that room but to the generations of believers who will come after. Thomas believed because he saw the wounds. But there will be many, Jesus says, who believe without seeing what Thomas saw — and they are blessed. Not disadvantaged, not operating at a deficit, not second-tier believers who have to make do with less evidence. Blessed. The faith that receives the testimony of the witnesses without requiring personal appearance is honored, not pitied.

Which is us. We have not seen the wounds. We have not touched the side. We have the testimony of those who did — and we have the Spirit who confirms that testimony in the interior of the believing heart (1 John 5:6–8), and the witness of the word that does not change, and the community of those who have been saying since that locked room: we have seen the Lord. And we are told we are blessed for receiving it. Our faith is not a lesser thing than Thomas's. It is the thing Jesus specifically commended.

For Reflection
  1. Jesus came back specifically for Thomas — to the place of his doubt, with exactly the evidence he requested. Is there a doubt you carry that you have not yet brought directly to Christ, assuming he would not or could not address it? What would it look like to bring the specific conditions of your unbelief to him as Thomas did?
  2. Thomas's deepest doubt produced his most direct confession: "My Lord and my God." Have you found that the places of your own deepest wrestling with Christ have sometimes produced the clearest faith? What does Thomas's story suggest about the relationship between honest doubt and genuine belief?
  3. "Blessed are those who have not seen and yet have believed." You are the person Jesus was speaking about in this beatitude. How does it land to know that your faith — held without having seen the wounds, without having been in that room — is specifically called blessed by the risen Christ? Does this feel like your experience of faith, or does something in you resist it?

Going Deeper

Thomas's confession — "My Lord and my God" (John 20:28) — is addressed directly to Jesus, and Jesus receives it without correction. In first-century Jewish context, this is theologically explosive. For a Jew to address a human being as "my God" would be blasphemy unless the claim were true. Jesus does not say "Thomas, you are going too far" or "that title belongs to the Father alone." He receives the confession, affirms it implicitly, and redirects it into the beatitude for those who will believe without seeing. John places this confession at the literary climax of his Gospel — the last and fullest answer to the "Who is this?" question that has driven the whole narrative. The Gospel opens with "the Word was God" (1:1) and closes with a human being addressing the risen Christ as "my God." The symmetry is deliberate: the whole Gospel is a demonstration of the truth that the Word who was God became flesh, and that the flesh which was crucified and buried rose and is recognized as God.

The Reformed tradition's insistence on the knowability of God through Christ — that in seeing and knowing the risen Jesus we are genuinely knowing God, not just a representative of God — is grounded precisely in this kind of exchange. Thomas is not wrong to call Jesus "my God." He is right, in the fullest possible sense. And the faith that receives this — across the centuries, without having been in that locked room — is the faith that John wrote his whole Gospel to produce: "these are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Christ, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name" (John 20:31). The Gospel is itself the testimony of an eyewitness, offered to those who were not present, so that we might receive what Thomas received — not the wounds to touch, but the confession to make. My Lord and my God.
