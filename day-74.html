Day Seventy-Four

Breakfast on the Shore

John 21:9–13, 15–17  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, you built a fire on the shore and cooked breakfast and called them to come. You fed them again — the same hands that broke bread in the upper room, the same care that fed five thousand, the same ordinariness of meal and fire and bread and fish. And then you turned to the one who had denied you three times and restored him three times, asking only the one question that mattered: do you love me? We bring that same question today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"When they got out on land, they saw a charcoal fire in place, with fish laid out on it, and bread… Jesus said to them, 'Come and have breakfast.'… Jesus said to Simon Peter, 'Simon, son of John, do you love me more than these?'… He said to him, 'Yes, Lord; you know that I love you.' He said to him, 'Feed my lambs.'… He said to him a third time, 'Simon, son of John, do you love me?' Peter was grieved because he said to him the third time, 'Do you love me?' and he said, 'Lord, you know everything; you know that I love you.' Jesus said to him, 'Feed my sheep.'"
John 21:9, 12, 15, 17, ESV

The risen Christ has appeared twice to the gathered disciples. Peter has heard the testimony and believed, has seen the grave clothes folded, has received the breath of the Spirit (John 20:22). And yet he does something in John 21 that suggests the weight of what happened at the charcoal fire of the high priest's courtyard has not yet been fully addressed: he says "I'm going fishing" — and goes back to Galilee, back to the boat, back to what he knew before. Whether this is obedience to the angel's instruction to meet Jesus in Galilee (Matthew 28:7), or a return to the familiar while waiting for what comes next, or something heavier — a man returning to what he was before he failed — John does not say. But the risen Christ meets him on the shore.

The detail of the charcoal fire is precise and deliberate. Anthrakian — a charcoal fire — appears only twice in all the New Testament. Here, and at the high priest's courtyard where Peter stood warming himself before he denied Jesus three times (John 18:18). John is not using the same word by accident. The risen Christ has arranged the setting to echo the scene of the failure: a fire, the shore in the early morning, the disciples present. He is going to address what happened at the other fire, and he is doing it in a context of ordinary care — fish cooking, bread ready, come and eat.

He does not begin with the restoration. He feeds them first. The meal is prior to the commission; the care precedes the question. When the meal is finished he turns to Peter and asks three times, in correspondence with the three denials: Do you love me? The first two questions use agapaō — the self-giving love of total commitment. The third, Jesus drops to phileō — the word for friendship, affection, the love of personal fondness. The shift at the third question is the one that grieves Peter: it is as if Jesus is meeting him at the level of love Peter actually has, rather than the level Peter claimed to have ("even if I must die with you, I will not deny you" — Matthew 26:35).

Each time Peter confesses love, he receives a commission: feed my lambs, tend my sheep, feed my sheep. The failure is not met with a reduction of trust. It is met with reinstatement to the same work, with a gentleness that acknowledges both the love that is there and the history that has happened between them. The restoration is threefold because the denial was threefold. Jesus is not just forgiving Peter in the abstract; he is walking back through the specific shape of the failure and replacing each denial with a confession and a commission.

The risen Christ who cooks breakfast on the shore for fishermen who have been out all night, who feeds them before he commissions them, who meets Peter at the level of the love Peter actually has rather than demanding the level Peter claimed — this is the shape of his restoration. Not "you have failed and here are the consequences." Not "I knew you would do this and I had a backup plan." Do you love me? Three times. And three times the answer, wounded and honest and real: you know everything, Lord. You know that I love you.

He does know. And he feeds the sheep anyway.

For Reflection
  1. The risen Christ met Peter at the scene of a failure and restored him not by ignoring the failure but by walking through it specifically, threefold. Is there a specific failure in your relationship with Christ that has not yet been walked back through — that you have accepted general forgiveness for but have not yet sat with him at the fire over? What would that conversation need to look like?
  2. He fed them before he commissioned them — the care came before the question. What does this order tell you about the basis of Christian service? Is your serving coming from a place of having been fed, or from a sense of obligation or debt that the feeding has not yet reached?
  3. "Lord, you know everything; you know that I love you." Peter's final answer is an appeal to Christ's omniscience — he has run out of words and is throwing himself on being known. Is there a prayer you cannot fully articulate — a love or a grief or a longing too deep for speech — that this kind of appeal might hold? What does it mean that he knows everything, including that?

Going Deeper

The threefold restoration of Peter in John 21 mirrors the threefold denial in John 18 so precisely — including the repeated use of a charcoal fire as the setting — that the structural parallel is obviously intentional on John's part. The risen Christ is not simply forgiving Peter; he is enacting a kind of narrative healing, walking back through the shape of the failure so that each specific moment of denial is covered by a specific moment of confession and commission. This is consistent with the pastoral pattern of the whole Gospel: Christ addresses people in the particularity of their situation, not in the generality of category.

The question of the two Greek words for love — agapaō and phileō — has been much discussed. Some commentators (notably C.S. Lewis and others in the older tradition) read the shift as significant: Jesus descending to Peter's actual level of love rather than demanding what Peter cannot yet give. Others argue that John uses the two words interchangeably throughout his Gospel and that the shift is stylistic rather than semantic. The pastoral force is the same either way: the restoration does not require Peter to produce a level of devotion he cannot honestly claim. It receives what is honestly there, commissions it, and entrusts to it the care of the flock. The sheep are fed not by the shepherd's perfection but by his knowing and by his willingness to work through imperfect love rather than waiting for perfect love to arrive. This is the risen Christ's consistent pattern with his disciples: he takes what is actually there and makes it enough, rather than waiting for what we think he requires before he can use us.
