Day Seventy-Six

Seated at the Right Hand

Ephesians 1:20–23  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Father, Paul prays that we might know what is the immeasurable greatness of the power that raised Christ from the dead and seated him far above every ruler and authority and power and dominion. We have spent fifteen days in the passion and resurrection. Now lift our eyes to what came next. The one who was crucified outside Jerusalem is seated above every created thing. Let the scope of that reality reach us today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"…he raised him from the dead and seated him at his right hand in the heavenly places, far above all rule and authority and power and dominion, and above every name that is named, not only in this age but also in the one to come. And he put all things under his feet and gave him as head over all things to the church, which is his body, the fullness of him who fills all in all."
Ephesians 1:20–23, ESV

We enter the final section. For fifteen days we walked the Via Dolorosa and stood at the empty tomb and followed the risen Christ through the appearances. Now, as Paul lifts our eyes to the present reality of the ascended Lord, we are asked to hold together everything we have seen across seventy-five days — the eternal Son, the servant in Galilee, the teacher on the mountain, the King over storms and death, the Lamb who was slain — and to see it all as the biography of the one who is, right now, seated at the right hand of the Father in the heavenly places.

Paul's language in Ephesians 1 is deliberately superlative. He stacks the qualifications: far above all rule and authority and power and dominion. Above every name. In this age and the age to come. All things under his feet. Head over all things to the church. He is doing what a writer does when ordinary description fails and only accumulation begins to gesture at the reality: he keeps adding. And the effect is not exaggeration but precision — an attempt to convey that the Lordship of Christ is genuinely total, that there is nothing above it, nothing beside it, nothing outside its reach.

The phrase "seated at the right hand" carries the specific weight of Psalm 110:1 — "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool'" — the psalm quoted more often in the New Testament than any other Old Testament passage. Sitting at the right hand of a king was the position of co-regent, the one who exercises the king's authority in the king's name. Christ is not merely honored in heaven; he governs. The right hand is not a recliner; it is a throne. He is currently ruling — pressing his authority across the cosmos, directing history, superintending the mission of his church, interceding for his people, holding all things together in the hand that bears the nail marks.

And then the breathtaking inversion at the end of the passage: all of this — the head over all things, the one above every power — is given specifically as head to the church. The same immeasurable power that raised him is the power available to the community of those who are in him. Paul's prayer is not merely that the Ephesians would know about Christ's Lordship in the abstract; it is that they would know it as theirs, as the resource of the body of which they are members. The church does not have access to Christ's power because it has managed to earn or leverage it. It has access because he was given as head to it — because the union between Christ and his people is so organic that what belongs to the head belongs to the body.

"The fullness of him who fills all in all." The church — imperfect, scattered, often faithless, sometimes foolish — is called the fullness of the one who fills everything. Not what the church achieves but what Christ fills it with. Not what it deserves but what he gives to it. This is the ground from which Christian witness is sent: not from impressive strength or cultural dominance or moral superiority, but from being joined to the one who sits far above all these things and fills those who are his with his fullness. You are a member of the body of the Lord of the cosmos. The ascended Christ is your head. This is the position from which the next fifteen days are read.

For Reflection
  1. Paul prays that the Ephesians would know the immeasurable greatness of Christ's power — as a matter of personal, experiential knowledge, not just doctrinal information. What is the gap between what you know about Christ's reign and what you live from? What would it look like to close that gap?
  2. "Far above all rule and authority and power and dominion" — there is nothing above Christ's authority in this age or the next. What authorities in your own life — cultural, political, psychological, relational — have functioned as if they were above or beyond his reach? How does this passage reposition them?
  3. The church is described as "his body, the fullness of him who fills all in all." You are a member of the body of the Lord of creation. Does this identity feel real to you — or does the imperfect and struggling reality of the church you know make this claim feel overstated? What would it take for it to become a living conviction rather than a theological affirmation?

Going Deeper

Psalm 110:1 — "The LORD says to my Lord: 'Sit at my right hand, until I make your enemies your footstool'" — is the single most quoted Old Testament text in the New Testament, cited or alluded to over thirty times. Jesus himself quotes it in the controversies at Jerusalem (Matthew 22:44), Peter quotes it at Pentecost to establish that the resurrection is the enthronement of the Davidic Messiah (Acts 2:34–35), and the letter to the Hebrews builds its entire Christology around it. The repetition is significant: the early church identified this psalm as the clearest Old Testament anticipation of the resurrection-ascension-enthronement of Jesus, and read it as the interpretive key to understanding what the resurrection meant — not merely that Jesus survived death, but that in rising he was publicly invested with the universal authority that had always belonged to him as the eternal Son.

The phrase "until I make your enemies your footstool" introduces the already-not-yet tension that governs all New Testament eschatology. Christ is seated — his authority is established, his victory is accomplished, his reign has begun. And yet his enemies are not yet visibly, finally under his feet. The final defeat of death, sin, and Satan awaits the consummation (1 Corinthians 15:25–28). Between the enthronement and the consummation, the church lives in the tension of a battle already won and a victory not yet fully revealed. The certainty of the outcome does not remove the reality of the present struggle; it recontextualizes it. We fight not toward an uncertain victory but from a certain one — not to secure what Christ has not yet accomplished, but to announce and embody what he has already secured. The King is on his throne. The battle is real. The end is never in doubt.
