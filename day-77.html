Day Seventy-Seven

The Lamb in the Midst of the Throne

Revelation 5:6, 9, 12  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, John wept because no one was found worthy to open the scroll. And then the elders said: stop weeping — the Lion of Judah has prevailed. John looked, and saw a Lamb as though it had been slain. The Lion is the Lamb. The conqueror bears the wounds. The throne of heaven is occupied by the one who was executed outside Jerusalem, and all of heaven cannot stop singing about it. Open our eyes to see it with John. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"And between the throne and the four living creatures and among the elders I saw a Lamb standing, as though it had been slain… And they sang a new song, saying, 'Worthy are you to take the scroll and to open its seals, for you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation'… 'Worthy is the Lamb who was slain, to receive power and wealth and wisdom and might and honor and glory and blessing!'"
Revelation 5:6, 9, 12, ESV

John is weeping. He has just watched one of the elders declare that the Lion of the tribe of Judah has prevailed — and he turns to look for the Lion, expecting the kind of power that conquers: mane, roar, force. What he sees is a Lamb, standing in the center of the throne, bearing the marks of slaughter. The Lion and the Lamb are the same. The form of the triumph is the form of the sacrifice. He conquered by being killed. He reigns bearing the wounds that secured the reign.

We have reached the place that John 5:5 describes as the destination of the whole — the Lamb at the center of heaven, worthy to open what no one else can open, worshiped by every creature in creation. This is the same Lamb that John the Baptist pointed to at the Jordan (Day 5): Behold the Lamb of God who takes away the sin of the world. From the river to the throne. From the announcement in the mud to the worship of ten thousand times ten thousand. The one who was pointed at is now the one everything else orbits.

The song the living creatures and elders sing is the new song — the one that could not be sung before Golgotha, that had no basis before the cross, that belongs exclusively to the age inaugurated by the Lamb's death and resurrection. Worthy are you to take the scroll, for you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people for God from every tribe and language and people and nation. The basis of his worthiness is the cross. Not merely his divine nature — though that is the ground of everything — but the specific, irreversible, historical act of being slain and purchasing people with his blood. Heaven sings about the cross. The throne of the universe is occupied by someone with wounds.

The scope of the ransomed people — every tribe, language, people, and nation — is the fulfillment of every promise made to Abraham, every vision of the prophets, every hint of universality embedded in the particular story of Israel. The one who went to the cross was going for everyone. The song sings it: no people group is outside the purchase, no language has been excluded from the invitation, no tribe is beyond the reach of what the Lamb accomplished. The Great Commission of Matthew 28 is grounded in this scene: go to all nations because the Lamb has already ransomed from all nations — the mission is to announce and invite into what has already been accomplished.

Seven things are declared worthy to be given to the Lamb: power, wealth, wisdom, might, honor, glory, and blessing. The sevenfold list suggests completeness, totality — every category of excellence and worth, everything that creation has to offer, is declared to belong to him. This is not merely the acknowledgment of what he has earned. It is the recognition of what has always been true and is now openly visible: he is worth everything, and everything is his.

We began Day 1 with the Word made flesh, the eternal Son entering time. We are four days from the capstone. The whole arc has been moving here — to the Lamb who stands in the center of the throne, who was slain and is now worshiped, who holds in one hand the scars of Calvary and in the other the scroll of history. He has not changed what he is. He has revealed it fully. And the appropriate response to seeing him as he is, is to join the song.

For Reflection
  1. John expected a Lion and saw a Lamb — the same Person, but the form of his power is sacrifice rather than force. What does it mean that the throne of the universe is occupied by a wounded Lamb? How does this shape your understanding of what power looks like in the kingdom of God — and in your own life?
  2. The basis of the Lamb's worthiness is "you were slain, and by your blood you ransomed people" — his cosmic authority rests on his atoning death. Heaven will sing about the cross forever. Do you find yourself returning to the cross as the center of your worship, or does it tend to become background? What would it mean to make it as central to your daily life as it is to the worship of heaven?
  3. The ransomed come from every tribe, language, people, and nation. You are one of them — ransomed from among the people and language where you were born, drawn into a company that no national or ethnic category defines. How does your membership in this universal community change the way you see the church locally, globally, and historically?

Going Deeper

Revelation 5 presents the climactic answer to a question that runs through the whole Bible: who has the authority to direct history toward its appointed end? The sealed scroll is the book of history — the narrative of creation moving toward its consummation — and the question is who has the right and the power to open it. No creature qualifies. Only the Lamb who was slain. The cross is not merely the means of individual salvation; it is the cosmic hinge on which all of history turns. Because the Lamb was slain and ransomed people for God, he has the authority to bring history to its appointed conclusion. The atonement and the eschatology are inseparable.

The Reformed tradition's understanding of Christ as Prophet, Priest, and King (Day 14) finds its ultimate expression in this scene. He is the Prophet who opens the sealed revelation (the scroll). He is the Priest whose sacrifice is the basis of his worthiness. He is the King enthroned above all powers in this age and the next. All three offices converge in the single image of the Lamb standing at the center of the throne. And the worship of Revelation 5 is therefore not one-dimensional praise of a distant deity; it is the specific, historically grounded adoration of the one who accomplished the specific, historical act that makes adoration possible. They sing because he was slain. The wounds are the reason for the worship. And they will be the reason forever.
