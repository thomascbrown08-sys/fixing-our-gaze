Day Seventy-Eight

Our Advocate

1 John 2:1–2  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, John writes these words not to give us license to sin but to give us a resting place when we have sinned: we have an advocate with the Father. Right now. You are pleading our case, not as someone who hopes the Father will be merciful, but as the one whose sacrifice is itself the argument — and the argument never runs out of force. Let this truth reach the places in us where we most feel accused today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"My little children, I am writing these things to you so that you may not sin. But if anyone does sin, we have an advocate with the Father, Jesus Christ the righteous. He is the propitiation for our sins, and not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world."
1 John 2:1–2, ESV

John writes to people who have been with Jesus since the beginning — who have heard and seen and touched the Word of life (1 John 1:1). He writes to people who know who they are and what they have received. And knowing all of that, he writes: I am writing so that you may not sin. The indicative precedes the imperative: because of who Christ is and what he has done, therefore do not sin. This is not legalism; it is the logic of gratitude. But John also knows — because he has watched these same people for decades — that not sinning is not a description of daily Christian experience. So he adds: but if anyone does sin.

The word "advocate" — paraklēton — is the same word Jesus used in John 14–16 for the Holy Spirit: the Helper, the Counselor, the one called alongside. Here it is applied to Jesus in his heavenly, ascended role: he stands beside us before the Father as our legal representative. The image is forensic — a courtroom, an accuser, a defendant who needs someone to speak on their behalf. The accusation is real: we have sinned. The record is accurate. And the advocate does not argue that the accusation is false.

He argues something better: the debt has been paid. The penalty has been absorbed. There is no outstanding claim against the one whose sins have been propitiated. He is the propitiation for our sins — the mercy seat, the place where wrath meets sacrifice and the two are reconciled — and his intercession before the Father is not a petition for leniency but a presentation of the completed atonement. He does not say to the Father "please be patient with them." He says "it is finished. They are covered. Look at the wounds." And the Father who sent him looks, and the accusation fails, not because it was incorrect but because it has been fully addressed.

The Roman legal system that formed the backdrop of John's world knew the advocatus — the friend at court who appears on your behalf, who knows the law and knows the judge, who can speak into the situation with a kind of authority and access that the defendant, standing alone, could never manage. Jesus is our advocatus before the heavenly court. He knows the law — he gave it. He knows the Judge — he is one with him. He knows the accused — he was tempted in every way they were. And his argument, presented at the right hand of the Father, is the only argument that has ever permanently closed a prosecution: I paid for this. It is finished.

John adds that the propitiation is "not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world." The sufficiency of the atonement extends to every human being — the Advocate's sacrifice was adequate for all, and his advocacy is available to all who come through him. This does not mean every person is automatically reconciled to God regardless of faith; it means the atonement is not a limited resource that might be exhausted before it reaches you. No one who comes to the Advocate will find that the case has already been argued and the supply of propitiation is spent. The throne room is open, the Advocate is at work, and the sacrifice that grounds the intercession is inexhaustibly sufficient.

For Reflection
  1. The Advocate's argument before the Father is not "please be lenient" but "the debt is paid — look at the wounds." How does knowing that Christ's intercession is not an appeal for mercy but a presentation of completed atonement change the way you approach God after you have sinned? Does it change anything about the quality of your confession or your confidence in approaching him?
  2. John writes these words "so that you may not sin" — the intercession is meant to produce holiness, not license. How does knowing you have an Advocate motivate obedience rather than complacency? What is the difference between those two responses to the same truth?
  3. The propitiation is "not for ours only but also for the sins of the whole world" — sufficient for everyone who comes. Is there someone in your life who seems beyond the reach of Christ's advocacy — too far gone, too hardened, too long away? What does this verse say to that assessment?

Going Deeper

The word hilasmos — propitiation — in 1 John 2:2 (and 4:10) has been at the center of significant theological debate. Some translations render it "atoning sacrifice" (NIV) or "expiation" rather than "propitiation," to avoid the implication that God's wrath needed to be appeased — as if God were an angry deity requiring satisfaction before he could love. The distinction matters. Expiation is the removal of sin from the sinner's record. Propitiation is the satisfaction of a just claim against sin. The Reformed tradition, following Leon Morris's careful lexical work, insists that hilasmos carries the propitiatory sense — not because God is capricious or vindictive, but because sin genuinely calls forth God's holy wrath, and the atonement genuinely addresses that wrath rather than merely overlooking it.

The pastoral importance of this distinction is significant. If the atonement is merely expiation — a cleansing of the record without reference to the justice that the record violated — then God's forgiveness is a form of divine looking-away, a gracious decision to ignore what should not be ignored. But if Christ's death is propitiation — the full satisfaction of divine justice by the one who bore the penalty in our place — then forgiveness is not a compromise of justice but its completion. God is "just and the justifier of the one who has faith in Jesus" (Romans 3:26): both things simultaneously, without remainder, because the atonement satisfied justice at its root. The Advocate's intercession rests on this foundation. He does not plead for a suspension of justice. He presents the evidence that justice has been done.
