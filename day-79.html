Day Seventy-Nine

A Priest Forever

Hebrews 7:24–25  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, every priest in Israel died and had to be replaced. Their intercession was intermittent, their lives were brief, their access to the Most Holy Place was limited to one day a year. You hold your priesthood permanently, because you live forever. You always live to intercede for those who draw near to God through you. We draw near today through you, to you, bringing what we cannot carry alone. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"He holds his priesthood permanently, because he continues forever. Consequently, he is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him, since he always lives to make intercession for them."
Hebrews 7:24–25, ESV

The letter to the Hebrews is an extended argument for the finality and superiority of Jesus Christ — greater than angels, greater than Moses, greater than the Levitical priesthood. And the heart of its argument about the priesthood is not ceremonial but temporal: the Levitical priests served and then died, and were replaced by sons who served and died and were replaced in turn, in an unbroken succession of mortality. The very repetition of the priesthood was evidence of its incompleteness. If one priest were sufficient, he would never need a successor. That the succession was endless was itself the testimony that what was needed had not yet been fully accomplished.

Jesus holds his priesthood permanently. Not for a term, not until a successor is found, not subject to the interruption of death. He continues forever. The priesthood that ended at the cross — the Levitical system of daily and yearly sacrifices, the high priest entering the Most Holy Place once a year and coming out again — was replaced not by a new version of itself but by the one to whom it had always been pointing. He offered himself once (Hebrews 7:27), and that one offering was sufficient and final. And then, instead of returning behind a veil to wait for the next Day of Atonement, he sat down at the right hand of the Father — the posture of completed work — and continues his priesthood in the mode of intercession.

He is able to save to the uttermost those who draw near to God through him. The phrase "to the uttermost" — eis to panteles — carries both a temporal and a qualitative meaning. Temporal: completely, all the way through, not partially or provisionally but to the final end. Qualitative: thoroughly, without remainder, leaving nothing of the need unaddressed. The salvation he provides is not a down payment on something that might or might not be completed depending on subsequent circumstances. It is total, terminal, final. And it is available to everyone who draws near to God through him — not to those who have achieved sufficient holiness to approach on their own, but to those who come through the one who is himself the way.

The grounds of this salvation are stated explicitly: since he always lives to make intercession for them. He does not intercede occasionally, on the days when the intercessory burden moves him. He always lives to do this — it is the present, continuous, permanent activity of the ascended Christ. Right now, at this moment, while you are reading these words, he is interceding for you. Not recalling your case from memory, not reviewing the file, not summoning the compassion needed for yet another petitioner. He always lives for this. His life at the right hand of the Father is a life of perpetual, active, personally engaged intercession for those who are his.

Charles Spurgeon was once asked how he could have any peace given his awareness of his own failures and sins. His answer was that he took comfort in knowing that even as he slept, Christ was at work on his behalf. The priest who never rests, who never goes off duty, who never hands the case to a successor — this is the ground of Christian peace. Not the believer's performance, not their consistency, not their ability to hold on tight enough. The permanence of the priesthood. He continues forever. He always lives. He never stops.

For Reflection
  1. "He always lives to make intercession for them" — right now, continuously, without interruption. What difference does it make to your prayer life to know that your prayers are not arriving at a distant throne unaided but are being carried and presented by a permanent, living, priestly advocate who never rests from this work?
  2. "Able to save to the uttermost" — temporally complete and qualitatively thorough. Is there an area of your life or history where you have doubted whether Christ's salvation reaches — a sin too old, a pattern too entrenched, a damage too deep? What does "to the uttermost" say to that specific place?
  3. The Levitical priests' need for successors was evidence of incompleteness. His permanent priesthood is evidence of completion. Where in your spiritual life are you operating with the anxiety of someone who is not sure the work will be finished — not sure the priest will outlast the need? How does his permanence address that anxiety?

Going Deeper

The letter to the Hebrews develops the most sustained argument for the Christological fulfillment of the Old Testament priesthood in the entire New Testament. Its key move is to identify Jesus as the priest of the order of Melchizedek — the mysterious figure who appears briefly in Genesis 14 and who Psalm 110:4 declares will be the model for the Davidic king's eternal priesthood ("You are a priest forever after the order of Melchizedek"). Melchizedek appears in Genesis without genealogy, without recorded birth or death — "without father or mother or genealogy, having neither beginning of days nor end of life" (Hebrews 7:3). He is a type of the eternal priesthood that Christ holds: a priesthood not derived from lineage (as the Levitical priesthood was) but from the power of an indestructible life.

The practical implication that Hebrews draws is stated in 4:14–16 and forms the devotional ground of the whole letter: "Since then we have a great high priest who has passed through the heavens, Jesus, the Son of God, let us hold fast our confession. For we do not have a high priest who is unable to sympathize with our weaknesses, but one who in every respect has been tempted as we are, yet without sin. Let us therefore with confidence draw near to the throne of grace, that we may receive mercy and find grace to help in time of need." The permanence of his priesthood is not merely a theological datum to be affirmed; it is a ground of access to be used. Come boldly. He is there. He always lives. And he is sympathetic, not distant — the high priest who has been exactly where you are and who holds his office permanently for the express purpose of helping those who draw near.
