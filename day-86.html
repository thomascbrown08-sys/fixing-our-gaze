Day Eighty-Six

He Who Calls You Is Faithful

1 Thessalonians 5:23–24  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, Paul closes his letter to the Thessalonians with a prayer that God would sanctify them completely and keep them blameless until the coming of Jesus Christ — and then the ground: he who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it. The guarantee is not our sanctification-achievement. It is his faithfulness. We rest in that today. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Now may the God of peace himself sanctify you completely, and may your whole spirit and soul and body be kept blameless at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ. He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it."
1 Thessalonians 5:23–24, ESV

Paul has been writing to a young church in Thessalonica — a community formed quickly, in circumstances of opposition, by people who turned from idols to serve the living and true God and to wait for his Son from heaven (1 Thessalonians 1:9–10). He is not writing to mature theologians or long-established believers. He is writing to people relatively new to the faith who are in a world that does not share their faith, navigating pressures and losses and questions that their new identity in Christ is still learning to answer.

And he closes the letter with a prayer and a promise. The prayer is total: sanctify you completely, keep your whole spirit and soul and body blameless. Not partial sanctification that leaves some corners of the life unaddressed. Not a spiritual condition that floats above the body while the body does what it likes. The whole person — spirit, soul, body — kept for the coming of Christ. The ambition is complete. The scope is everything. This is what God is working toward in every believer: a wholeness that matches the whole Christ who indwells and intercedes and will one day return for what he has claimed.

And then the guarantee that makes the prayer credible: He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it. The faithfulness is the ground. Not your faithfulness — though the letter has called for faithfulness at every point. Not your spiritual productivity — though the letter has described what productive faith looks like. The faithfulness that guarantees the completion of what God has begun is his own character. He cannot be unfaithful without ceasing to be God. He has called you — that call is his act, his initiative, his investment — and having called you, he will bring to completion what the calling began.

This echoes what Paul wrote to the Philippians: "I am sure of this, that he who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ" (Philippians 1:6). The one who started is the one who finishes. The completion is not your achievement appended to his beginning; it is his work from first to last, with your genuine participation and response at every stage, but with his faithfulness as the guarantee that the response will be there and the work will be done.

The Thessalonian context gives this particular flavor: these are people whose faith is costing them something, who are facing afflictions (1:6; 3:3–4), who have lost some of their number to death before the return of Christ and are grieving and uncertain (4:13–18). Paul is not writing to comfortable people. He is writing to people who need to know that the faithfulness of God is not dependent on the stability of their circumstances. He will surely do it — not if the circumstances cooperate, not if the suffering relents, not if things go reasonably well. He will surely do it.

Four days from the capstone. Carry this into them: the one who called you is faithful. Whatever the state of your faith today — strong or weak, assured or uncertain, growing visibly or struggling in a plateau — he who called you is faithful, and he will bring to completion what he began in you. That is the promise. He will surely do it.

For Reflection
  1. "He who calls you is faithful; he will surely do it." The guarantee is his faithfulness, not yours. Where do you most need to transfer the responsibility for your sanctification from your own performance to his faithfulness? What would that transfer actually look like in practice?
  2. Paul prays for complete sanctification — spirit, soul, and body — not merely a spiritual improvement that leaves the physical life untouched. What does it mean to you that God's sanctifying work extends to your body, your habits, your physical life? Is there something embodied that you have kept outside the reach of his sanctifying work?
  3. "He who calls you is faithful." He called you. That call was his act, his initiative. When did you first hear the call — what was the shape of it? And knowing now that the faithfulness of the one who called is the guarantee of the completion, what does looking back at the calling do for your confidence about what lies ahead?

Going Deeper

The prayer of 1 Thessalonians 5:23 raises a question that Christian anthropology has addressed from many angles: what does it mean to be sanctified "completely" in "spirit and soul and body"? Paul's threefold description — pneuma, psychē, sōma — has been read both as a trichotomous view of human nature (spirit, soul, and body as three distinct components) and as a rhetorically emphatic way of saying "the whole person, without remainder." The Reformed tradition has generally favored the second reading, understanding Paul to be emphasizing comprehensiveness rather than delineating three separate parts. What matters theologically is the totality: God's sanctifying work reaches every dimension of human existence, not only the "spiritual" parts that feel most susceptible to religious influence.

The phrase "at the coming of our Lord Jesus Christ" — en tē parousia tou kyriou hēmōn Iēsou Christou — introduces the eschatological horizon of sanctification. Sanctification is not merely a this-life project of incremental improvement; it aims at and is completed by the parousia, the arrival of the Lord. This is why 1 John 3:2–3 can connect the hope of his coming directly to purification: "When he appears, we shall be like him, for we shall see him as he is. And everyone who thus hopes in him purifies himself as he is pure." The coming of Christ is both the goal of sanctification and the motivation for it. We are being made ready for the one who is coming — and the one who is coming is the one who is making us ready. The faithfulness that guarantees the completion is the same faithfulness that is even now doing the work of completion, from the inside out, by the Spirit, one degree of glory at a time.
