Day Eighty-Seven

Come, Lord Jesus

Revelation 22:17, 20  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord Jesus, the last prayer in the Bible is a single sentence: Come, Lord Jesus. Not "help me manage until you come" or "sustain me in the absence." Come. It is the prayer of someone whose gaze has been fixed long enough on the one who is coming that the desire for his actual, physical, final arrival has become the deepest prayer of their heart. Three days from the capstone. Come, Lord Jesus. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"The Spirit and the Bride say, 'Come.' And let the one who hears say, 'Come.' And let the one who is thirsty come; let the one who desires take the water of life without price… He who testifies to these things says, 'Surely I am coming soon.' Amen. Come, Lord Jesus!"
Revelation 22:17, 20, ESV

The Bible ends with a conversation between the coming Christ and the waiting church. He says he is coming soon. She says come. And the movement is bidirectional: the invitation of the Spirit and the Bride calling all who are thirsty to come and drink freely, and the longing of those who have drunk to see the one from whom the water flows arrive in person at last.

Maranatha — the Aramaic word behind "Come, Lord Jesus" — is one of the oldest recorded prayers of the early church (1 Corinthians 16:22; Didache 10). It predates the writing of Revelation by decades. From the very beginning, those who had seen the risen Christ and been sent back into the world by him were praying this prayer: Our Lord, come. Not as a desperate prayer of people who had lost hope, but as the prayer of people who had seen enough of him to want the seeing to be complete — who had received the firstfruits and were longing for the full harvest.

The prayer is possible only because the gaze has been fixed. A person who has not been looking at Jesus — who has not spent time in his presence, who does not know what it is to feel the warmth of his love and the weight of his glory — will not pray this prayer with genuine desire. The prayer comes from the heart that has been formed by beholding. You cannot long for the arrival of someone you have not met. You can only long for the arrival of someone whose presence you have already tasted and found incomparably good.

This is what eighty-seven days of fixing the gaze is for. Not to produce doctrinal competence, not to satisfy a religious obligation, not to accumulate spiritual capital. But to grow the longing. To increase the desire. To turn the heart more and more toward the one who is coming, until the prayer that was once a formula becomes a genuine cry: Come, Lord Jesus. Not because this world is so bad it is unbearable — though there is real suffering here — but because he is so good that nothing this side of his full presence is sufficient. The devotional is a mechanism for longing-formation. The ninety days are not the destination. They are the shaping of the desire that points toward the destination.

He says: Surely I am coming soon. The word tachu — quickly, soon — has puzzled readers for two thousand years. How is it "soon" if it has been two millennia? But Peter addresses this: "With the Lord one day is as a thousand years, and a thousand years as one day. The Lord is not slow to fulfill his promise as some count slowness, but is patient toward you, not wishing that any should perish" (2 Peter 3:8–9). The soon is not a failed prediction; it is a different kind of time. He is coming, and from his perspective, the coming is imminent. Every generation of believers since the resurrection has been the last generation — has been right to live with the posture of those whose Lord might arrive before morning. That posture is not anxiety; it is expectation. The kind of expectation that makes you careful about what you are doing when he arrives, and glad when you imagine it.

Amen. Come, Lord Jesus. The last prayer of the Bible is the right prayer for the last days of this devotional. Whatever comes next — tomorrow, the final three days, the rest of your life — come from this: the gaze fixed, the longing real, the prayer alive in the mouth: Lord, come. We are ready for you. We have been getting ready. Come.

For Reflection
  1. "Come, Lord Jesus" is the prayer that grows from sustained beholding — you can only long for someone you know. Has this devotional grown the longing in you? Is "Come, Lord Jesus" a prayer you can pray with genuine desire — not as escape from this world, but as the natural cry of a heart that has tasted him and wants the full feast?
  2. The Spirit and the Bride say "Come" — and in the same breath invite all who are thirsty to come and drink without price. The longing for Christ's return and the welcoming of those who have not yet come are simultaneous. How does the expectation of his coming shape the urgency with which you hold out the invitation to those around you?
  3. "Surely I am coming soon." He has promised. Two thousand years have passed and the church has lived in every generation as those for whom the coming might be imminent. What does it mean to live with that expectation — not as an anxiety about timing, but as a posture of readiness? What would look different in your life if you lived today as someone for whom the coming of the Lord is genuinely the next event on the horizon?

Going Deeper

The prayer Maranatha — "Our Lord, come" — appears in 1 Corinthians 16:22 in an Aramaic form preserved within a Greek letter, suggesting that it was so well-established in early Christian worship that Paul could use it without translation, confident that his Gentile readers in Corinth would recognize it. This is remarkable: Aramaic was the language of the Palestinian Jewish Christians, not of the Gentile churches. The fact that the prayer had crossed this linguistic boundary suggests it was among the earliest and most universal elements of Christian worship — present from the very beginning, shared across the communities that had been formed by the resurrection appearances.

The prayer expresses what the Reformed tradition calls the "blessed hope" (Titus 2:13) — the joyful, confident expectation of the return of Christ that should characterize the whole of the Christian life and not merely its dying moments. Calvin's comment on the closing of Revelation is characteristic: "By this prayer we declare that all our desires and our whole heart should be turned toward the coming of Christ." The return of Christ is not merely an item on the eschatological timeline; it is the event that gives the present its meaning and direction. The church that knows what is coming can endure what is present — not because the present is unimportant, but because the one who is coming has already told them how it ends. The last prayer of the Bible is, in this sense, the most forward-looking sentence ever written: it looks past everything — past the suffering and the uncertainty and the incompleteness — and sees him. Coming. Soon.
