Day Eighty-Nine

He Makes All Things New

Revelation 21:1–5  ·  English Standard Version
Opening Prayer

Lord, John saw a new heaven and a new earth, and the holy city coming down from God like a bride adorned for her husband, and he heard a great voice from the throne saying: God himself will be with them as their God. He will wipe every tear. Death will be no more. Everything sad is coming untrue. One day more before the capstone. Let us receive this vision today with the full weight of everything we have hoped for across eighty-nine days. Amen.

Today's Scripture
"Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth… And I heard a loud voice from the throne saying, 'Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. He will dwell with them, and they will be his people, and God himself will be with them as their God. He will wipe away every tear from their eyes, and death shall be no more, neither shall there be mourning, nor crying, nor pain anymore, for the former things have passed away.' And he who was seated on the throne said, 'Behold, I am making all things new.'"
Revelation 21:1, 3–5, ESV

This is the world that is coming. Not the vague afterlife of popular imagination — disembodied souls floating in a spiritual ether, freed at last from the inconvenience of physical existence. A new heaven and a new earth. New Jerusalem coming down from God. The entire created order, renewed and perfected, as the dwelling place of the God who will live among his people not in a temple behind a curtain but in direct, unmediated, unveiled presence. The most ancient human longing — for home, for belonging, for the end of all that is wrong — met at last, comprehensively and permanently, by the God who made the longing in the first place.

The voice from the throne says: Behold, the dwelling place of God is with man. The Greek word — skēnē — is the tabernacle word, the same word behind John 1:14 where the Word "tabernacled" among us. The full circle is now visible: from the first tabernacling in the Incarnation — God coming to dwell among his people in the flesh of Jesus of Nazareth — to the final tabernacling in the new creation, where God will dwell with his people not in a body that can be arrested and executed but in the fullness of his presence, filling the new creation the way water fills the sea.

He will wipe every tear. The gesture is intimate beyond what formal theology usually reaches. Not "all tears will be wiped" — the passive, impersonal construction that suggests an administrative process. He himself will wipe every tear. His hand, on every face, on every specific grief. The one who wept at Lazarus' tomb (Day 22) will be the one who wipes the tears at the end of all things — still personal, still attending to the particular, still knowing each face and each tear's name. Every loss, every grief, every wound that seemed too deep to heal — addressed, personally, by the one who entered suffering and came out the other side carrying it into the new creation where it will be finally, permanently undone.

Death shall be no more. This is the sentence that changes everything. Every other form of loss in human experience is amplified by the knowledge that it ends in death — that the people you love will die, that you will die, that even if everything else goes well the ending is the same. The new creation removes this amplifier. The last enemy is defeated (1 Corinthians 15:26), and with it every lesser enemy that borrowed its power from the threat of final ending. No more mourning, no more crying, no more pain. The former things have passed away.

And then, from the throne, the word that makes all of this possible: I am making all things new. Present tense. Not "I will make" or "I have made." Making — active, present, continuous. The renewal of all things is not a future event that has not yet begun; it is the work that has been in progress since the resurrection, since the Spirit was poured out, since the first person was born again and given a new heart. The new creation has already begun, in the hearts of those in whom Christ dwells, and it will be complete when he who sits on the throne brings the work to its conclusion.

He makes all things new. Including you. Including the parts of your story that seem most irreversibly broken, most thoroughly lost, most resistant to redemption. He makes all things new. He began the work on Easter morning. He is doing it now. He will finish it when he comes.

For Reflection
  1. "He will wipe away every tear from their eyes" — personally, specifically, attending to each face and each grief. What is the tear you most need him to wipe? Not as a theological aspiration but as a specific loss or grief you carry — can you name it, and receive the promise that it is included in "every tear"?
  2. "I am making all things new" — present tense, begun already, not yet complete. Where in your own life do you see the first signs of the new creation already at work — places where something old and broken is being replaced by something new and whole? What does it mean that this is the same power that will one day renew all things?
  3. One day remains. You have spent eighty-nine days fixing your gaze on Jesus Christ — from before the foundation of the world to the throne of the new creation. What is the single most important thing these days have given you? And what are you most hoping for from the final day?

Going Deeper

The vision of Revelation 21 is not the annihilation of the present creation and its replacement by something entirely discontinuous. The Greek word for "new" — kainos — typically denotes newness of quality rather than newness of origin: not a completely different thing, but the same thing made fresh, renewed, restored to and beyond what it was meant to be. This is consistent with the New Testament's broader vision of the consummation: Paul speaks of creation being "set free from its bondage to corruption" (Romans 8:21), not dissolved and replaced. Peter speaks of the present heavens and earth being "dissolved" (2 Peter 3:10–13), but his conclusion is that we are waiting for "new heavens and a new earth" — the same language as Revelation 21, pointing to renewal rather than replacement.

This matters enormously for Christian engagement with the present world. If the new creation is the renewal of this creation rather than its abandonment, then what is done in this world matters — it has some connection, however transformed and glorified, to what persists into the next. Paul's word at the end of 1 Corinthians 15 — the great resurrection chapter — concludes with this: "Therefore, my beloved brothers, be steadfast, immovable, always abounding in the work of the Lord, knowing that in the Lord your labor is not in vain" (15:58). The reason labor is not in vain is that the resurrection changes its destination. The work done in and for the kingdom has a permanence that the work done merely for this present age does not. He makes all things new — and in the making, what has been given to him is not lost but transformed into what it was always meant to become.
