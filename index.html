Fixing Our Gaze

A Ninety-Day Devotional Journey into the Person of Jesus Christ

"Let us run with endurance the race God has set before us. We do this by keeping our eyes on Jesus, the champion who initiates and perfects our faith." — Hebrews 12:1–2, NLT

What This Devotional Is For

Fixing Our Gaze exists for a single, repeated purpose: to look at Jesus Christ. Not merely to accumulate doctrines about him, or to marshal arguments on his behalf — though both matters have their rightful place — but to behold the Person himself. The Church throughout the centuries has discovered, again and again, that sustained and attentive contemplation of Christ is itself the great engine of spiritual transformation. What we look at most determines what we become. And there is no sight more worthy of our attention, our wonder, and our worship than the one whom the ancient creeds confess as "very God of very God, begotten not made, of one substance with the Father."

This devotional is designed for everyone: seekers who are beginning to fix their eyes on Jesus for the first time, and seasoned believers who have looked at him long enough to know there is always more to see. It is written with the conviction of Timothy Keller's insight — that the gospel faithfully proclaimed illuminates the Christian further into Christ's riches while simultaneously showing the seeker something real and compelling about what saving faith looks like in action. Every day here invites both responses.

The ninety days trace a loose arc: from the eternal nature and glory of the Son, through his incarnate ministry and teaching, through his death and resurrection, to his present reign and our coming hope. But each day is also a facet — a single, distinct face of the diamond held up to the light — and can be read alone, in whatever sequence life provides.

How Each Day Is Arranged

Each day opens with an Opening Prayer — not a formality, but a posture. Before anything else is said, we come as those who cannot see on our own, asking for eyes to behold what is really there. The prayer is meant to precede not only the reading, but the day itself.

A single Scripture passage follows, drawn from across the whole Bible in a variety of trusted translations. The passage is not an occasion for comprehensive exegesis; it is a window. We are standing at the window, looking through.

The Meditation (~700 words) draws on the riches of Christian history — voices like C.H. Spurgeon, A.W. Pink, John Owen, Herman Bavinck, Dietrich Bonhoeffer, John Stott, N.T. Wright, and Tim Keller — to illumine what Scripture reveals of Christ. These voices appear not as authorities over Scripture but as fellow gazers, pointing at what they have seen.

Reflection Questions invite you to move from reading to responding — from observation to application, from the page to the life. And a Going Deeper section provides theological grounding for those who want to understand the doctrinal foundation beneath what they have read. It is optional but rewarding.
